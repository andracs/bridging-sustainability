# 10. Appendix: checklists for sustainable IT and sustainable digitalization

Here in the appendix you can find a summary of the rules of thumb, advice and practices that we have presented in the book. We have gathered them together to help you get an overview of the tools you can use when working with sustainability in your digital practice. The checklists are a suggestion of things you should be aware of in IT and digitalization projects. They are not exhaustive, but they are prioritized so that the first actions are expected to have the greatest impact. The checklists can serve as inspiration for your - and your organization's - sustainability journey, as they are based on the best practices that we have observed through our research and practice. The checklists are published under an open source license, and they can be copied, modified and with advantage expanded and supplemented with additional actions.

Overarching design principles for sustainable digital solutions 🌱💻📘✨♻️🌍
This list can serve as inspiration for overall sustainability considerations when developing digital solutions.
[Active link to the Karlskrona Manifesto preserved in e-book]
1.	Sustainability encompasses environment, technology, economy, individual and society
The production and operation of digital products should not be environmentally and climate friendly, but also have a positive impact on society and individual users (see also: Karlskrona Manifesto).
2.	Design circular processes
"The 'use and throw away' culture is rarely sustainable; circular economy is a better approach. Digital products should have a cradle-to-cradle lifecycle with a goal of close to 0% waste.
3.	Renewable energy is best
Digital solutions should be based on renewable energy as much as possible. Explore opportunities for suppliers and buyers to also use renewable energy.
4.	Use agile development methods
Sustainability in IT is still a blank slate, which is why iterative development with long-term goals and short iterations is often preferable.
5.	Take responsibility for the full product lifecycle
Companies should take responsibility for how their products are produced, used and disposed of, and how they affect users, the environment and society. This applies to both physical and digital products.
6.	Follow recognized standards and certifications
There are a number of sustainability standards for software, hardware and networks, with more under development. Consider obtaining certifications that can support your organization's goals. This can boost your market position in markets where sustainability is in demand.
7.	Give users sustainable choices
Software should be designed to inspire and help users to make increasingly sustainable choices.
8.	Low energy consumption is better than high energy consumption
Choose solutions with lower direct and indirect energy consumption to minimize the solution's CO2 emissions.
9.	Less is more
Small and simple solutions often use less energy and resources than large solutions. Consider whether downsizing or lightweight design might be viable strategies.
10.	Cybersecurity and resilience is also sustainability
Solutions that can be hacked or abused are not sustainable. Make sure you have high IT security standards.
11.	Avoid greenwashing
Base your work on widely recognized and scientifically supported sustainability principles to avoid greenwashing.
💾♻️ Sustainability principles for software
This checklist guides you in developing or procuring software solutions with a focus on sustainability in a broad sense.
🌱 1. Think long term
Build sustainability into the software from the start, rather than including it as an add-on or an afterthought. Include data sources in the design that promote sustainability.
🌐 2. Follow well-established sustainability best practices
We don't have to reinvent the wheel, because we can build on established standards and practices, such as
[Links are preserved and active in e-book]
•	The Karlskrona Manifesto for sustainable design
•	W3C's Web Sustainability Guidelines
•	The Blue Angel certification for software
•	Application Security Verification Standard (ASVS)
•	B Corp Certification.

🚫 3. Avoid unsustainable practices
Software should be conducive to both the individual and organizations. Avoid dark patterns that can be harmful to software users. Also, avoid creating technical debt by using technology that is not long-lasting. Instead, use components that are open to development and future-proof.
🤖 4. Automate sustainability analytics
[Links must be preserved and active in e-book]
Ensure that automatic measurements benchmark the software's effectiveness on an ongoing basis for both environment and safety. Find tools for this at e.g:
•	Green Metrics Tool
•	Green Software Tools on GitHub .
🧑‍💻 5. Guide users towards sustainability
Help users make more sustainable choices that benefit both themselves and the future. Have sustainable defaults and use nudging to change user behavior in more sustainable directions. Make sure users are happy - also in the long run.
💡 6. Manage data energy efficiently
Build facilities into the software that can promote energy-efficient data management. Be economical and frugal with the computational resources required.
📚 7. train developers continuously in sustainable practices
All employees should have a basic understanding of the importance of sustainability in general, and different job roles can benefit from more specific training or online learning.
💾 8. Follow the good old virtues from the long history of software development
The history of information technology is a loooooong chain of optimization and improvement processes that can contribute to more sustainable software practices. For example:
1.	Reduce unnecessary computing because frugal solutions require less energy and less electronics need to be produced to run them.
2.	Monitor resource consumption and environmental impact to make informed decisions about future improvements.
3.	Use efficient and proven algorithms or you'll be in technical debt for the future.
4.	Design for modularity and reusability to save on development costs.
5.	Make sure the software is open to expansion and integration to allow different solutions to work together and create new or more efficient products.
6.	Have a plan for continuous updates, both because it's essential for the security of the software, but also because it can create a better business.
7.	Make the software easy to troubleshoot and provide appropriate documentation , it pays off in the long run.
8.	Consider optimizing for older hardware because backwards compatibility can pay off in some cases.

🌱💻 Sustainability principles for the use of hardware
This checklist helps you assess hardware solutions with a focus on sustainability. Use it as a practical tool to ask the right questions and make informed choices that benefit the environment, society and also your organization.
🏷️ 1. What sustainability standards does the hardware meet and what certifications does it have?
Choose hardware that is certified according to standards that ensure responsible production.
🌿 2. Is the hardware produced according to sustainability best practices?
•	Use sustainable raw materials and recycled components.
•	Ensure end-of-life waste sorting is responsible.
•	Work with suppliers who prioritize good working conditions and renewable energy.
⚡ 3. Is the hardware energy efficient compared to competitors?
Compare the energy consumption and performance of different products and choose the most efficient.
⏳ 4. What is the life expectancy of the hardware ?
Prioritize long-life hardware , but consider technological improvements that can justify upgrades.
🔧 5. Is the hardware repairable ?
Make sure spare parts are available and that hardware can be upgraded and repaired to extend its lifespan.
♻️ 6. How to ensure 100% recycling of obsolete hardware?
Choose suppliers that offer responsible disposal and recycling of end-of-life products.
🚀 7. Future aspects of the hardware
Check if the hardware can be upgraded or reused for future needs.
🌍 8. How does the supplier see hardware developing in the coming years?
Prioritize suppliers that work with innovative solutions to reduce environmental impact.

🔌🌍 Sustainability principles for network and cloud
Use the questions below to set the right requirements for suppliers and solutions - from energy-efficient hardware to the use of renewable energy and intelligent systems. The goal is to ensure environmentally friendly operations without compromising on safety and quality.
🌱 1. Which sustainability standards and certifications does the solution have?
Choose suppliers that work with transparent sustainability goals and possibly also certified according to applicable environmental standards. Suppliers' ESG reports can provide information on this.
⚡ 2. Is the network based on energy-efficient and environmentally friendly hardware?
Work with hardware designed for low energy consumption and choose products with minimal environmental impact.
☀️ 3. Is the operation based on renewable energy?
Make sure that both local and global data centers are powered by renewable energy like solar and wind, or that they capture as much CO2 as they emit.
❄️ 4. Is the data center design environmentally friendly in terms of cooling?
Prioritize data centers that use free cooling or other energy-saving cooling systems.
🤖 5. Does the data center have intelligent solutions to minimize the environmental footprint?
Is it possible to automatically turn off resources when they are not in use? Automated systems that optimize resource usage are preferable.
🔐 6. Is security in order?
Does your network and cloud meet the best possible security standards and applicable legislation such as GDPR or NIS2?
