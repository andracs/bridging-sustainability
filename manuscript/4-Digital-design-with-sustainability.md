# 4. Digital design with sustainability

This chapter explores how to design and develop digital solutions that effectively fulfill their intended purpose while also achieving long-term sustainability, guided by the five dimensions of the Karlskrona Manifesto.

## Design That Does Good – Also in the Long Term

Digital projects typically originate from an idea, evolving through design, engineering, and innovation into tangible solutions or products. The design phase is pivotal, as it establishes many of a product's fundamental characteristics and lays the groundwork for its entire lifecycle. Consequently, integrating sustainability considerations, framed here by the Karlskrona Manifesto's five dimensions, during this early stage is essential. This ensures sustainability is an inherent, deeply embedded quality, not merely a superficial layer added retrospectively. It's also a fact that it's cheaper and more efficient to design a digital product with sustainability built in from the start than trying to rebuild the product in a more sustainable direction later.

While the allure of technical possibilities can be strong, it's easy to lose sight of the core purpose during development. Yet, all digital projects are ultimately created *for* people and *with* people. Therefore, the design phase must remain resolutely focused on the intended users and their experiences. How do people actually interact with a prototype? Does it evoke a sense of genuine value and usefulness ("Wow, that's clever!"), or does it feel like yet another cumbersome system adding friction or monotony ("Oh, another unnecessary system...")? ## Sustainable design approaches

_Digital transformation_ is the process of integrating digital technologies into all aspects of a business to improve efficiency, customer experiences and innovation. Digital transformation involves a fundamental evolution in business processes, culture and customer interactions to gain competitive advantage. It can go hand in hand with the green transition, because if we manage to combine the two development approaches, significant synergies can emerge between them. New digital solutions should therefore be designed so that they can also be sustainability solutions.

Digital design often focuses on people, for whom digital technology can offer great user experiences and better productivity. But when we focus on the needs of people in design, we tend to forget about nature, the environment, society and the other dimensions of sustainability. Good digital design focuses all the way around. Let's look at some sustainable design approaches: "Design for the New World" and "Life centered design".

### Design for the New World: thinking generations ahead

In the first chapter of the book, you could read about the planetary boundaries that human activities are feared to exceed. As a response to this threat, designer Ida Engholm in her book _Design for the New World_ has defined a sustainable approach to design that can change the way we design products - analog and digital (Engholm 2023).

Engholm's sustainable design approach adds an extra "planetary" dimension  to the design work, taking into account the earth's ecosystems and future generations. The design approach can lead the way for more sustainable digital development projects, projects that help bring planetary systems into balance. However, thinking on a planetary scale can seem overwhelming for us humans when our task is simply to develop new digital solutions for a company. Nevertheless, it is the way forward, says Ida Engholm.

Figure 4.1 shows how simple design decisions on a micro level can contribute to the bigger, planetary perspective, with the overall aim of looking after the planet. In this case, it's a single person (maybe you?) or organization that replaces a non-degradable plastic component with a degradable one in a product. The difference this single action makes is not easy to understand in the big picture - but it still contributes to "global sustainability", as this plastic component does not end up as waste like many others, but degrades and re-enters nature's own cycle . The figure makes it possible to understand both the overall context of the planet (e.g. that plastic does not belong in the ocean, as it does not just magically disappear from the oceans), and it is also possible to understand sustainability on a micro level (that we must use plastic components that can be broken down).

If you think back to the definition of sustainability from the Brundtland Report, sustainability is about acting in a way that does not jeopardize the opportunities of future generations. This mindset can be found in Engholm's book, where she is inspired by the Native Americans, who have a tradition of considering decisions from a seven-generation perspective. Engholm believes that we should do the same: design products from a long-term perspective. By zooming out in time, we can better understand our product lifecycle and create more circular designs.

Engholm's design approach has many other details that this book doesn't have the space to cover, but you can take some points as guidelines for your digital design work:

- Consider[[A1]](#_msocom_1) [[A2]](#_msocom_2)  the consequences of your product's impact for generations to come.
- Consider how your digital design can impact the planet on a macro level.
- Design for the entire product lifecycle (cradle to cradle).

### Life-centered design

Another inspiring design approach can be found in the work of Australian designer Damien Lutz. Lutz is an advocate of life-centered design , which is a design philosophy that, in addition to the needs of users, puts all living things in focus. Life-centered design aims to care for all living things and for more biodiversity and more nature as a result of the design process. Lutz's take on a life-centered digital design practice is to alternate between many different design approaches, depending on the qualities of the design task, in order to truly deliver sustainable results.

In the article "10 life-centered design approaches" (Lutz 2023) he presents a selection of different design approaches that you can have in your toolbox when designing life-centered. Common to all approaches is that they include the living environment in their design considerations. For example, _circular design_ can be used if you want to take responsibility for a product's life cycle , while _inclusive design_ is the solution if the challenge is to involve as many users as possible in the product, including user groups that are traditionally excluded from using a product.

Not content with simply applying the design approaches of others, Lutz also offers his own take on a "life-centred design framework" (Life-Centred Design Lab 2024). The framework's starting point is to ensure that the design of a product takes into account all its users, all animals and the entire planet - an ambitious and noble goal. Lutz argues that you can work towards these goals by basing your design on three pillars. The design must be:

●      Inclusive and pluriversal[[1]](#_ftn1)

●      Responsible and aligned

●      sustainable and regenerative.[[2]](#_ftn2)

It's about creating design that is accessible to all users, regardless of background, needs or cultural context. Design should be diverse and reflect different perspectives and worldviews. Design that is "responsible and aligned" focuses on ethical responsibility, where the process takes into account the consequences of the choices made, both socially and environmentally. The final pillar is about creating products that not only limit environmental damage, but also actively improve and rebuild the ecosystems they impact.

And to get that broad perspective, the designer must continuously zoom in and out in their views on three scales. You therefore need to look at your design:

●      locally and globally

●      from a user perspective and as a lifecycle

●      from the past and in the future.

Life-centered design balances between the local and the global perspective to ensure that the product works both in the local context and also on a global scale. In addition, the design must also make trade-offs between a good user experience and consideration for the full life cycle of the product , while building on lessons learned from the past and creating solutions for the future.

This design framework is just one of many, but it illustrates many of the qualities that a sustainable digital designer must master: thinking long term, circular processes and embracing both individuals and communities in the design process.

## User experience (UX) design

UX design (user experience design ) focuses on creating digital products that provide meaningful and relevant experiences for users. Working with UX involves gaining insight into user needs and behavior through empathic analysis, experimentation and user testing. The purpose of UX is to make the experiences of products intuitive, efficient and satisfying - on a technological, economic and individual level. UX is an effective method for increasing user satisfaction, but it also has the inherent weakness that it can focus one-sidedly on the (selfish) needs of the individual and downplay the sustainability dimensions (e.g. social, economic or technical) of the product.

UX design uses tools such as wireframes, prototypes, user testing, personas, user journeys and other tools to understand and improve the user experience. These tools are used to try to predict and model what experiences users want from a product or product portfolio.

"Personas" are fictional characters that represent typical users and their needs, goals and behavioral patterns, as shown in the left side of figure 4.2. The creation of personas is based on user research and data. These fictional people are described very specifically with name, age, occupation, hobbies and preferences, with the goal of giving the designer an indication of who will interact with the products. Decisions in the design process are continuously weighed up against these personas to assess how the different design initiatives will affect the customers' user experiences.

According to UX designer Sandy Dähnert from the company Green the Web, the basic work with personas can be extended by creating non-human and non-user personas that can represent the sustainability aspects of the product (as exemplified on the right side of Figure 4.2). Dähnert has added Mother Earth, a metaphor for the globe, as a persona in his design process (Dähnert 2021a). The purpose of the exercise is for the designer to test the impact of their design ideas on nature. Lutz provides further examples of non-human personas, such as the bees (in danger of extinction), the trees or a child laborer from the third world, exemplified by the persona of a 12-year-old child laborer (Lutz 2023).

### Sustainable user journeys

[Link in footnote 17 and 18 be active in e-book]

Both Dähnert[[4]](#_ftn4) and Lutz[[5]](#_ftn5) are working to develop, apply and disseminate eco-centered UX methods, another example being _sustainable user journeys_ . The user journey is a common tool in the UX designer's toolbox because it helps to view user behavior from a more holistic perspective and helps to understand how the user comes into contact with a digital product through many different _touchpoints ._

An example of a user journey can take place around an application that a user first sees on a social media (first touchpoint). Then the user will discover the app in ads, after which the user chooses to install the app from an app store (further touchpoints). The user actively uses the app for two years, after which it is forgotten. After a year of inactivity, the app is automatically uninstalled and the user journey ends unless the digital designer adds additional touchpoints to the user journey.

By mapping the user journey through the digital and also analog landscape, designers can gain a better understanding of the user's situation, motivation and needs. Dähnert's innovative idea is to expand the common user journeys with sustainability aspects (Dähnert 2021b). This is done by further analyzing the user's different touchpoints from an ESG perspective and looking at how a given touchpoint affects the environment and society. Dähnert focuses on positive and negative impacts in relation to the environment and society, but you can also extend the analysis to include the sustainability dimensions of the Karlskrona Manifesto.

UX design is about creating meaningful and engaging experiences by deeply understanding user needs and behavior . Sustainable UX design goes a step further and focuses not only on the user, but also on the sustainability aspects of the digital product. By extending the UX designer's tools such as personas and user journeys to include sustainability aspects, we open up for a more holistic design process. Although we have only presented sustainable versions of personas and user journeys in this context, the other tools in the designer's toolbox can also have sustainability add-ons. These could be wireframes and templates that have inclusive and energy-saving defaults, or user testing that takes into account the entire product lifecycle from cradle to cradle.

These innovative UX approaches show how common UX tools can be extended to drive user-friendly and eco-friendly solutions and inspire the creation of products that make a difference for both people and the planet.

### UX design with data

Data can be a catalyst for digital sustainability projects. Digital designers need to be aware of what data already exists, what data can be collected and what data is not yet available. The more detailed an overview a designer has access to, the more innovatively data can be brought into play. Digital design helps create economic value, but it can also be used to discover connections between data and sustainability.

In some contexts, the designer can draw on enormous data sources, also known as _big data_, which with advanced algorithms and machine learning can provide features to a digital product that would be unthinkable without the designer's in-depth knowledge of relevant data sources.

That's why it's beneficial in the design phase of digital products to focus on topics such as:

●      _Data discovery_, where you find pre-existing datasets and analyze their relevance to the design project.

●      _Data integration_, involving different data sources to get a holistic picture of user behavior and preferences, as well as sustainability aspects of the product.

●      _Data wishlist_ , describing wishes for new data sources that can improve the digital product.

●      _Data collection_, building mechanisms into the product for continuous (but of course ethical) data collection to continuously improve the product.

●      _Internet of Things (IoT_) , which allows large amounts of data to be collected with various digital sensors to improve the product.

Data collection is a sensitive topic that should give rise to many ethical and practical considerations.

You must comply with current legislation such as the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which regulates what the collected personal data can be used for. You should also make sure to only collect data that is necessary for the different sustainability dimensions that the designer is working with. The ethics of data work should be emphasized to ensure that data use benefits both present and future generations without causing negative effects.

Working with data is a unique discipline, but all UX designers should consider how new and existing data can be used to benefit both users and sustainability.

## Interaction design (UI)

While UX is about understanding user behavior and creating good user experiences, interaction design is about designing good and effective user interfaces (UI: User Interface). A good user interface makes the experience of a digital product intuitive, useful and engaging. Interaction design incorporates elements of graphic design and adds interaction techniques such as nudging to guide users towards desired actions. UX and UI are disciplines that together can create coherent and effective user experiences, combining an understanding of the user with design principles that promote desired behavior .

There are several approaches to interaction design that also consider sustainability in design, such as sustainable interaction design (SID) or sustainable HCI (sustainable human computer interaction). These directions focus on developing interactive solutions with consideration for environmental and social impacts. It's not just about reducing energy consumption or waste, but also about creating systems that support long-term responsible use, promote positive behavioral patterns and contribute to a more sustainable future. What these approaches have in common is that they examine the interaction between people, software and hardware and try to make the interaction as sustainable as possible. An example of thinking about sustainable HCI can be found in Eli Blevis' article (Blevis 2007), in which he discusses sustainable interaction design with a focus on the development, disposal, renewal and reuse of technology. For Blevis, design must take responsibility for the full life cycle of products , from creation to disposal. This approach is most relevant if the interaction has a materiality, such as a technological gadget or a touchscreen, that needs to be disposed of, and the approach can only be applied to pure software products to a limited extent.

### Design patterns for web solutions and apps

It's not immediately obvious how graphical user interface design can be made more sustainable, but this is certainly an area that can benefit from sustainability initiatives. First and foremost, designers must be aware of the existence of "dark patterns", which are user interactions that are inappropriate or downright bad. Unfortunately, it is a common practice for digital products to more or less mislead users in order to achieve company goals such as conversions. You would think that this practice is reserved for the most commercial companies, but we have also seen dark patterns in government and non-profit organizations.

Dark patterns , or deceptive patterns , are design patterns that have been developed and reused in many digital products over the years to coerce or incite users into certain situations with certain psychological tricks. Many of these techniques are described in the book _Evil by design_ (Nodder 2013), which contains a series of dark patterns that can effectively influence user behavior "in the dark", i.e. without the user realizing that he or she is being manipulated. We know many of these sales tricks from the commercial world, but digital products can make the deception even stronger. In the name of sustainability, we must avoid dark patterns - or at least use them ethically.

A concrete example of deceptive design is _infinity scrolling_, which is when a page keeps loading more content elements as a user scrolls. This way, the user can keep scrolling forever - never reaching the end or end of a web page. This can create additional consumption of the given media. That said, the same function can also be used ethically. For example, if you need to browse through many products in a webshop, it's a good feature that you don't have to click "Show more" after every tenth product. It will also make the process clearer if you make the user aware of how many items there are in the feed in total.

Another dark pattern you may have come across is _visual weighting_ or _choice architecture_ , which many websites use when the user has to accept their cookies. Here, the website often has an interest in imposing more cookies on the user than is strictly necessary because it serves the company's interests. Therefore, accepting cookies is often graphically designed in such a way that it is cognitively "cheap" to choose the dominant, color-saturated "Allow all" button, while the "Only necessary cookies" option appears as a weaker choice - with smaller font size and less contrast between text and background. However, the same graphical trick can be used sustainably, for example by making it easy for the user to make sustainable choices and making it difficult to act unsustainably.

### Web design and greenhouse gas emissions

Many digital designers are asking themselves and their networks how they can make their designs sustainable. It's a challenge, and many of the sustainability initiatives the industry is working on focus on reducing greenhouse gas emissions.

In recent years, many methods have been developed to calculate the emissions and carbon footprint of digital designs, but some of these methods can produce conflicting results. The field is constantly evolving, but we cannot ignore the fact that the energy consumption of the user interface itself in digital solutions is only a fraction of the total energy consumption, and most of the optimization should be done in the underlying systems.

For example, you retrieve data from a server when you visit a website. The server consumes a lot of energy, regardless of whether it receives requests or not, because it runs 24 hours a day. The underlying digital infrastructure thus accounts for a large part of the energy consumption in scope 1 and 2 of a digital solution. To this we must add the energy consumption of the network and all the devices that use energy 24/7 in order for you to access the website (e.g. your router or modem - and the many other network devices used for data transfer).

Only as the last link in the chain does the website appear on a screen. The energy consumption of the computer itself is also considerable, regardless of whether you visit the website or not. There is a significant energy consumption in a web request that is completely independent of the presented graphic design and optimization of the frontend (the design of the user interface), and therefore we must remember that when optimizing the graphic design and interactive user interface, we can only energy optimize the last few percent of the total energy consumption.

These connections are described in more detail in the Web Sustainability Guidelines (WSG)[[6]](#_ftn6)[[A3]](#_msocom_3) [[A4]](#_msocom_4)  by the World Wide Web Consortium, which brings together advice and guidelines for the development of web solutions that are both environmentally friendly and socially responsible. The document contains a collection of best practices that can minimize the overall digital carbon footprint of web solutions , optimize resource consumption and contribute to the long-term sustainability of web projects. WSG lists over 90 guidelines for user experience, web design, hosting and the digital business. Many of these guidelines align with the principles we present throughout this book, but unfortunately we can't include them all here. Instead, we have selected some key areas that the specification addresses:

**UX design**

·       Keep the design lightweight with well-worded content.

·       Offer a useful navigation system to users and use recognized (non-negative) patterns.

·       Work with design systems to reduce duplication.

·       Design for accessibility so that all user groups have great experiences.

·       Revise and test regularly.

**Web development**

·       Optimize your project by using techniques to eliminate unnecessary or duplicated code.

·       Make sure your project is accessible to all audiences and contains useful information.

·       Assess third-party services and program code for issues (and for value creation).

·       Fix programming errors as they occur.

·       Ensure a layout that adapts to different devices (e.g. mobile, PC and smart TV).

·       Include useful files that can be expected by browsers or search engines.

·       Use the latest versions of development tools.

**Hosting, infrastructure and systems**

·       Be frugal with computing resources and choose green hosting. Scale as needed.

·       Optimize all files and remember proper error handling.

·       Automate where you can.

·       Manage data processing and data storage with care.

**Business strategy and product management**

·       Designate a sustainability manager and offer sustainability training for others.

·       Understand your impact. Set goals. Report and publish progress.

·       Share benefits with your employees. Share decision-making with everyone.

·       Be ethical with sensitive information and technology (such as AI).

·       Offer an inclusive workplace. Also, give back to good causes and open source.

WSG unfolds the above principles in detail and provides concrete techniques that can contribute to less energy consumption and more sustainable approaches to web design. These techniques change year after year, so keep up to date with developments if you want to make a difference in web design. The same goes for the topic in the next section: "User interface optimization tools".

### Tools for optimizing user interfaces

There are a number of tools that digital designers can use to evaluate a web design from a sustainability perspective (climateaction.tech 2021). The tools can provide automatically calculated estimates of how the design performs - and they often come up with different results because there are no well-established standards in the field (yet). Some of the more notable tools are:

- _Website__[[A5]](#_msocom_5)_ [[A6]](#_msocom_6)  _Carbon Calculator_ that measures CO2 emissions from a website based on data usage and energy sources (see figure 4.3).
- _Ecograde_, which evaluates a website's environmental impact by analyzing its code, design and hosting.
- _Digital Beacon_, which assesses the sustainability of digital solutions by analyzing their energy consumption.
- _The Green Web Foundation_, which tracks the environmental impact of websites and promotes the use of green hosting providers.
- _Cabin Analytics_, which provides insight into a website's carbon footprint by analyzing data usage and server efficiency.
- _Greenspector.com_, offering a measurement and analytics solution to reduce the environmental impact of mobile and web applications.
- _Are my third parties green_, which checks the third-party resources used by a website.

These tools use different criteria and assumptions as a starting point for their estimates and their results vary widely at the time of writing. The same website can get very different evaluations from different tools because the results are often rough estimates and the tools are weighted differently. However, it's a good idea for digital workers to get to know these tools and apply them to their projects. They represent an optimization mindset that is conducive to sustainability. Some projects go a step further and only display demanding UI elements during periods of green power, while during periods of black power they will save on the UI (by graceful degradation ). Some websites even run solely on solar or wind energy and can therefore be turned off during periods without green power.

At the same time, it's important to remember the proportionality of environmental considerations, namely that digital products consist of complex technical solutions, and optimizing the user interface can usually only lead to marginal environmental improvements. So optimizing UX, UI and web design is part of the green digitalization puzzle, even if it's not where we find the biggest energy savings. However, every small change can contribute to an overall more sustainable digital future.

## Nudging - a greener user behavior

A less technical and more user-centered method in digital design is _nudging_ (DaSilva et al. 2022). Nudging is a method through which you can influence people's behavior . It is done through interactions and design elements that encourage users to make certain choices without limiting their options. By designing user experiences in a way that encourages certain actions, you can encourage people to make more sustainable or responsible choices. At its core, and as an ideal challenge, nudging can be thought of as a kind of positive feedback loop that reinforces "desired good behavior" in relation to health, economics and public policy. For example, showing users how their energy consumption ranks against the most energy-saving options to encourage them to save energy, or presenting healthy food choices to improve users' diets. However, don't be naïve. Nudging is a behavioral economics discipline and is quite often about securing a particular sale and increasing consumption of certain types of goods.

However, when nudging is used to promote sustainability aspects and is clearly communicated, it can be considered ethically sustainable. For example, it can be considered sustainable nudging if you highlight environmentally friendly products in your webshop by default. The same webshop can equip its products with clear sustainability labels to promote more sustainable choices. Or you can integrate into the user interface so that the customer can filter the items for local products.

## Summary: What can you take away from this chapter?

"Let's do more good and less harm with design_._" Damien Lutz

This chapter has looked at how digital design can contribute to a more environmentally friendly and responsible digital future. We have looked at different design approaches, such as Design for a New World and Life-centered design, and how they try to extend existing practices with sustainability aspects. We also touched on UI design, design with data and discussed the sustainability aspects of user interfaces and web solutions.

Digital design for sustainability is not only about reducing energy consumption and using environmentally friendly practices, but also about thinking long-term and including all aspects of a product's lifecycle in the design. The examples have shown how small changes in design can have a significant impact on the energy consumption and environmental impact of digital products.

We have also seen the importance of avoiding unsustainable design patterns (dark patterns) by bringing them to light and transforming them into sustainable patterns. Last but not least, it's worth noting that user-centered design tends to overestimate the interests of users and downplay the consequences for the outside world, but the tools presented will hopefully give you a good starting point for working with a more sustainable UX/UI in practice.

Let's make a difference, one pixel at a time.
