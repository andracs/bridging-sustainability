### Case Story: Combining heating and electrical energy storage in one solution 🔋

Across Europe, countless homes rely on electric heaters which burden power grids during peak times and add high costs to household budgets. Simultaneously, the shift towards renewable energy sources like solar power demands a more flexible, responsive electricity grid. French entrepreneur Raphaël Meyer saw this inefficiency not as a barrier, but as the inspiration to start Lancey Energy Storage and develop a smarter heating system in line with the European energy transition.

Raphaël Meyer pivoted from his research in photovoltaics in the 2010s towards energy storage, initially exploring sodium as a basis for batteries, which proved to be an insufficiently mature battery technology at the time. 

Meyer's core insight was that true energy efficiency often lies in turning unavoidable energy losses into useful outputs. Just as the heat from data center cooling can be used for heating nearby housing, the heat generated when charging batteries can be used for heating in a radiator. This led to the idea of integrating a battery into a radiator, using the battery's waste heat for room heating, thus boosting overall system efficiency.

Lancey developed a smart radiator with an integrated lithium-ion battery, which can be charged while electricity is cheap and can provide heat while electricity is expensive. This optimizes heating, stores cheap off-peak or surplus solar energy for high self-consumption, supports grid stability, reduces CO2 emissions from peak power plants, and lowers heating bills. Furthermore, it facilitates solar power use and self-consumption.

Despite developing an innovative, award-winning product and securing funding, Lancey faced business challenges and had to change track in 2025. However, the core vision of turning energy inefficiencies into business opportunities persists, highlighting the importance of entrepreneurial resilience required in sustainability-focused ventures.

Meyer is optimistic about the future of sustainable energy, and has many good ideas for the energy transition. His entrepreneurial vision for a future with renewable energy contains, among others, the following ideas:

1. "A society focused on seasonal production rather than just seasonal storage". He envisions doubling industrial capacity for materials like aluminum, steel, and concrete, running these facilities primarily during periods with plenty of renewable energy, effectively storing value in products rather than relying solely on batteries.
2. He also predicts that sooner or later, buildings will incorporate at least some battery storage, making it as common as other basic appliances.
3. Through local energy management and storage, buildings will gain control over their energy use, independent of when power is supplied by the grid.
4. An interesting goal for energy startups could be to provide their integrated heating and energy storage systems "as a service," eliminating the initial investment barrier for users and closing the loop on the value proposition.

But as he emphasizes, ideas alone are not enough to make a business. Operational details make the difference.

## Governing Sustainability 🏛️
What kind of opportunities do we have for governing digital projects in sustainable ways? First, we must consider whether we are aiming to create sustainable IT and support sustainable development, or whether we seek to enable sustainability through IT—also known as *"sustainability by IT."*

## Sustainable profits? 💰
Sustainability is the idea of conducting business in a way that can be maintained over the long term, without depleting natural resources or harming the environment. We might theorize that a sustainable company could be more resilient and robust in the long run compared to less sustainable businesses. But is that actually the case? Is sustainability truly good for business over time?

On one hand, we see that many unsustainable companies have grown large and profitable, so historically, sustainability has not been a prerequisite for success. On the other hand, there is growing research suggesting that sustainable companies may enjoy certain competitive advantages, such as improved brand reputation, customer loyalty, operational efficiency, and long-term risk management.

## Starting a sustainable digital business 🚀

**Digital entrepreneurship** broadly refers to the process of creating new or innovative internet-based companies, products, or services. This definition encompasses both startups introducing new digital offerings to the market and the digital transformation of existing business activities within established companies or the public sector. Digital entrepreneurship thus connects entrepreneurship with technological development, creativity, innovation, and the acquisition of digital skills. It involves shifting physical assets or services to the digital domain using electronic communication. The core of digital entrepreneurship lies in leveraging digital tools and platforms to create value and opportunities, which fundamentally differs from traditional, purely physical business models. This shift enables unprecedented scalability and reach, which has significant implications for the potential impact of sustainable solutions.

In the intersection between digital entrepreneurship and sustainable development, we find the concept of "digital sustainable entrepreneurship," which can be viewed as a business model perspective that integrates digital technologies with the aim of creating social and environmental value.

Europe has seen a wave of tech startups combining digital innovation with green causes - from energy management to circular economy. In fact, Europe is home to roughly twice as many *climate tech* startups as the US (30,000 vs. 14,300), despite struggling with limited venture funding ([World Fund](https://www.worldfund.vc/knowledge/white-paper-climate-tech-for-european-resilience)).

## Sustainable by IT 🌿
Digital technologies such as AI, IoT, and data analytics can help other industries reduce their CO2 emissions; analyses suggest that digitalization can enable a reduction of up to 20% of global emissions by 2030 ([Digital Europe](https://www.digitaleurope.org/resources/digital-action-climate-action-8-ideas-to-accelerate-the-twin-transition/#:~:text=Studies%20have%20found%20that%20by,of%20global%20CO%202%20emissions)). This makes tech entrepreneurs key players in the green transition, as they develop software, platforms, and hardware that improve efficiency and reduce environmental impact across sectors.

A recurring theme is that IT can optimize complex issues, a network of actors, considerations, etc. Dashboards can visualize and observe in real-time.

**[Next Kraftwerke](https://www.next-kraftwerke.com/)** in Germany is an example of a digital energy company; it connects thousands of decentralized wind turbines, solar cells, batteries, and consumers into one virtual power plant via a cloud platform ([Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/start-next-kraftwerkes-renewable-virtual-power-plant-stabilises-grid#:~:text=,lion%20share%20in%20power%20trading)). This "power plant without power plants" balances green electricity in the grid and has effectively replaced the capacity of two large coal-fired power plants, saving significant amounts of CO2 ([Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/start-next-kraftwerkes-renewable-virtual-power-plant-stabilises-grid#:~:text=,next)).

**Smart city**: Other examples include smart city and industrial solutions that leverage digital technologies for sustainability. In Vienna, an innovative smart city project in a residential block has reduced CO2 emissions by a staggering 71% using data and digital management systems ([Digital Europe](https://www.digitaleurope.org/resources/digital-action-climate-action-8-ideas-to-accelerate-the-twin-transition/#:~:text=For%20example%2C%20a%20smart%20city,to%20reduce%20its%20environmental%20footprint)).

In the Port of Rotterdam, artificial intelligence is used to optimize ship route planning and port calls, which is expected to halve the port's CO2 emissions by 2030 ([Digital Europe](https://www.digitaleurope.org/resources/digital-action-climate-action-8-ideas-to-accelerate-the-twin-transition/#:~:text=In%20the%20Port%20of%20Rotterdam%2C,their%20carbon%20emissions%20by%202030)).

Furthermore, an AI tool installed in a Belgian wind farm has increased the amount of renewable energy that can be delivered to the grid by approx. 5–6% ([Digital Europe](https://www.digitaleurope.org/resources/digital-action-climate-action-8-ideas-to-accelerate-the-twin-transition/#:~:text=Renewable%20energy%20is%20crucial%20to,6)).

These examples illustrate that digital solutions across sectors (construction, transport, energy, etc.) can in practice make the economy greener and more resource-efficient.

In the financial sector, we also see digital innovation supporting sustainability. Fintech startups are developing platforms for green investment, climate data, and *"impact"* measurement, which can steer capital towards sustainable purposes. A prominent example is the Swedish startup **Doconomy** (founded 2018), which has developed digital tools for banks and consumers that make it possible to measure the carbon footprint of financial transactions and make greener choices ([ESG Today](https://www.esgtoday.com/climate-impact-fintech-doconomy-raises-37-million-to-help-consumers-make-sustainable-choices/#:~:text=Founded%20in%202018%2C%20Doconomy%20has,transactions%2C%20enabling%20more%20sustainable%20choices)).

Doconomy's platform converts every payment on a credit card into a CO2 estimate and gives the user insight into how much their consumption burdens the climate. The solution has attracted large capital – in 2024, Doconomy raised approx. €34 million in an investment round to expand its climate-focused fintech products ([ESG Today](https://www.esgtoday.com/climate-impact-fintech-doconomy-raises-37-million-to-help-consumers-make-sustainable-choices/#:~:text=Swedish%20impact%20fintech%20company%20Doconomy,consumers%20make%20more%20sustainable%20choices)).

*Illustration:* Doconomy's **DO** card is the world's first credit card with a CO2 emission limit – it stops new purchases when the user's climate impact has reached a set ceiling ([Thales Group](https://www.thalesgroup.com/en/markets/digital-identity-and-security/banking-payment/inspired/doconomy#:~:text=It%27s%20the%20world%27s%20first%20credit,your%20footprint%20for%20the%20year)). The card is also made of bio-based, biodegradable material instead of traditional plastic, emphasizing the fintech sector's focus on sustainability in both digital services and physical products ([Thales Group](https://www.thalesgroup.com/en/markets/digital-identity-and-security/banking-payment/inspired/doconomy#:~:text=The%20company%20has%20put%20sustainability,impacting%20the%20environmental%20and%20social)). This combination of digital monitoring and green innovation has won international recognition and awards, showing how financial technology can engage citizens in the climate fight.

Sustainable finance also extends to established players. For example, the Dutch **Triodos Bank** has had sustainability as a core principle from its very beginning and exclusively finances projects and companies with a positive environmental or social impact. The bank publishes details of every single loan for full transparency ([Thales Group](https://www.thalesgroup.com/en/markets/digital-identity-and-security/banking-payment/inspired/doconomy#:~:text=The%20company%20has%20put%20sustainability,impacting%20the%20environmental%20and%20social)), and it offers customers a payment card made of bioplastic (PLA) – one of the most environmentally friendly bank cards on the market ([Thales Group](https://www.thalesgroup.com/en/markets/digital-identity-and-security/banking-payment/inspired/doconomy#:~:text=Beyond%20that%2C%20Triodos%20publishes%20details,it%20makes%20for%20total%20transparency)). These initiatives from both new fintech startups and traditional banks illustrate how the digitalization of finance can support the green transition by changing incentives for both investors and consumers.

## Digital solutions in the healthcare sector (MedTech) 🏥

The healthcare sector accounts for a not insignificant part of society's resource consumption and emissions, but digital innovations in *MedTech* (medical technology and health IT) can contribute to sustainability. **Telemedicine** – virtual doctor consultations via video/apps – is a clear example that both improves health access and reduces environmental impact. By replacing physical attendance, patients' transport is saved. A systematic review of 48 studies found that a total of ~68 million telemedicine consultations saved approx. 691,000 tons of CO2 and 3.3 billion kilometers of travel distance ([European Urology Focus](https://www.eu-focus.europeanurology.com/article/S2405-4569\(23\)00272-9/abstract#:~:text=A%20total%20of%2048%20studies,More)).

These CO2 savings correspond to taking a great many cars off the roads, thanks to digital health solutions. During the COVID-19 pandemic, telemedicine took a huge step forward; for example, the Swedish teleplatform **Kry** (also known as Livi) has delivered over 6 million online consultations since 2015 and facilitated 200+ million patient interactions in Europe ([Tech.eu](https://tech.eu/2022/02/08/with-6-million-patient-appointments-delivered-kry-now-looks-to-scale-specialist-services/#:~:text=specialist%20services)). This indicates that digital health can be scaled massively, which can both give patients easier access to medical help and potentially reduce the carbon footprint from transport to and from clinics.

The MedTech industry itself emphasizes that green and digital initiatives go hand in hand with better health. According to MedTech Europe (the European trade association), a closer link between climate and health policy can lead to health systems that prevent more diseases, minimize unnecessary interventions, and optimize patient pathways, increasing the number of healthy life years for citizens ([MedTech Europe](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf#:~:text=sustainable%2C%20equitable%20and%20resilient%20to,is%20providing%20safe%2C%20innovative%2C%20and)). At the same time, future health systems must be more resource-efficient and digitally supported. This involves, among other things, the use of smart hospital solutions, telemedicine, and data-driven prevention to reduce waste and emissions. In short, digitalization in the healthcare sector is seen as a key to achieving "green" gains without compromising patient treatment.

## Cases: European startups in the twin transition 🇪🇺

Several European startups and scaleups have already developed products that support both digitalization and sustainability. Some prominent examples include:

- **Too Good To Go (Denmark)** – A digital platform and app that fights food waste by connecting consumers with restaurants, bakeries, and supermarkets that have surplus food. In 3½ years after its launch in 2016, Too Good To Go had saved 29 million meals from the bin and thus avoided the emission of more than 72,000 tons of CO2 – equivalent to removing 15,000 cars from the roads for a year ([Zero Waste Europe](https://zerowasteeurope.eu/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/zero_waste_europe_CS7_CP_TooGoodToGo_en.pdf#:~:text=7%203%2C5%20years%20after%20saving,to%20expand%20year%20after%20year)). The concept has scaled to 14 countries and now "saves" approximately one meal per second via the app ([Zero Waste Europe](https://zerowasteeurope.eu/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/zero_waste_europe_CS7_CP_TooGoodToGo_en.pdf#:~:text=Too%20Good%20To%20Go%20has,to%20expand%20year%20after%20year)), showing how a digital business model can create tangible climate results.
    
- **Doconomy (Sweden)** – An *impact-fintech* startup (founded 2018) that gives consumers and companies tools to track and reduce their carbon footprint through financial data. Doconomy's mobile app and APIs convert payment transactions into CO2 emissions so users can see the footprint their purchases leave ([ESG Today](https://www.esgtoday.com/climate-impact-fintech-doconomy-raises-37-million-to-help-consumers-make-sustainable-choices/#:~:text=Founded%20in%202018%2C%20Doconomy%20has,transactions%2C%20enabling%20more%20sustainable%20choices)). The solutions include "DO-credits" and savings goals for sustainable behavior, and the company is expanding globally with support from major investors ([ESG Today](https://www.esgtoday.com/climate-impact-fintech-doconomy-raises-37-million-to-help-consumers-make-sustainable-choices/#:~:text=Swedish%20impact%20fintech%20company%20Doconomy,consumers%20make%20more%20sustainable%20choices)). Doconomy illustrates how fintech can engage citizens in sustainability and deliver data-driven insights for the green transition.
    
- **Next Kraftwerke (Germany)** – An energy-tech company (founded 2009) that operates one of Europe's largest *virtual power plants*. Through a digital platform, Next Kraftwerke has aggregated over 7,500 decentralized renewable energy units with a total capacity of almost 7,000 MW across Germany and neighboring countries ([Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/start-next-kraftwerkes-renewable-virtual-power-plant-stabilises-grid#:~:text=,lion%20share%20in%20power%20trading)). These units (wind turbines, solar parks, biogas plants, etc.) are managed and traded as one power plant via cloud software, enabling grid stabilization with green electricity. The virtual power plant reportedly replaces the need for two large coal-fired power plants and also delivers balancing services to the grid ([Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/start-next-kraftwerkes-renewable-virtual-power-plant-stabilises-grid#:~:text=,next)). Next Kraftwerke demonstrates how digitalization of the energy market can significantly increase the share of renewable energy and reduce CO2 emissions through smarter coordination.

*(The above are just a few examples – similarly, countless other European startups are seen in everything from agritech (precision agriculture) to green mobility and industrial symbiosis, all combining digital innovation with sustainability.)*

## Future prospects for the digital and green transition 🔮

**Innovation and Investment:** The EU's vision for 2030 contains ambitious goals for both digital transformation and green transition. Under the initiative *"Europe’s Digital Decade"*, concrete 2030 targets have been set, including that 75% of EU companies should use cloud, AI, or big data, that the number of European "unicorns" (valued startups > $1 billion) should double, and that 10,000 climate-neutral, highly secure edge data centers should be established around Europe ([European Commission](https://commission.europa.eu/strategy-and-policy/priorities-2019-2024/europe-fit-digital-age/europes-digital-decade-digital-targets-2030_en#:~:text=Tech%20up,Cloud%2C%20AI%2C%20or%20Big%20Data); [European Commission](https://commission.europa.eu/strategy-and-policy/priorities-2019-2024/europe-fit-digital-age/europes-digital-decade-digital-targets-2030_en#:~:text=Cutting%20edge%20Semiconductors%3A%20double%20EU,share%20in%20global%20production)). These digital goals go hand in hand with the EU's *Green Deal* objectives of 55% CO2 reduction in 2030 (relative to 1990) and climate neutrality in 2050. To ensure that investments follow ambitions, the EU's recovery fund (NextGenerationEU) requires that each member state's plan allocates at least 37% of the budget to green initiatives and 20% to digital initiatives ([European Commission](https://commission.europa.eu/business-economy-euro/economic-recovery/recovery-and-resilience-facility_en#:~:text=agreed%20maximum%20amount.%20,towards%20completing%20the%20reforms%20and)) – a concrete expression that the "twin transition" is thought of as integrated. Overall, it is estimated that the EU needs massive investments: up to €745 billion towards 2030 to finance the twin transition ([Digital Europe](https://www.digitaleurope.org/news/joint-statement-a-call-to-action-for-a-digital-single-market-by-2030/#:~:text=The%20Single%20market%20represents%20a,the%20twin%20transition%20until%202030)). A well-functioning single market strategy could even unlock a further ~€719 billion in economic growth over 5 years ([Digital Europe](https://www.digitaleurope.org/news/joint-statement-a-call-to-action-for-a-digital-single-market-by-2030/#:~:text=The%20Single%20market%20represents%20a,the%20twin%20transition%20until%202030)). In other words, there is great economic potential if capital can be mobilized for both digital and green projects.

The positive future forecast is that the twin transition opens new opportunities for innovation and growth. New startups within e.g. green AI, climate finance, energy storage, smart cities, and circular economy are expected to attract increased capital and attention. Digital solutions will become increasingly widespread as necessary tools to reach climate goals – from intelligent power grids and IoT sensors in production, to *blockchain* for tracking supply chains and carbon footprints. The education and labor market will also be affected: Europe is estimated to need at least 20 million ICT specialists by 2030 to drive the digital transition ([BFNA](https://www.bfna.org/politics-society/the-future-of-work-in-the-twin-transition-to-green-and-digital-3xn0a3queo/#:~:text=Digital%20www,to%20facilitate%20the%20digital)), reflecting a growing need for skills in both software development, data analysis, and green technology. If these skills are developed and retained in Europe, it can strengthen the region's competitiveness and lead to job creation in high-value sectors, while addressing climate challenges.

## Barriers and pitfalls 🚧

Although the potential is great, there are also well-known barriers and pitfalls that can slow down digital entrepreneurship in the sustainable transition. 

**Market pressure and economics:** One challenge is that green business models must be able to compete economically. Some promising green startups have struggled to achieve commercial sustainability, even with substantial funding. An illustrative example is the Berlin startup **Infarm**, which specialized in digitally controlled indoor farming (vertical farming). Infarm grew rapidly and raised almost $500 million in capital, but had to declare insolvency in 2023, as the business model could not sustain the high energy costs and the "green premium" price that indoor plant production entailed ([Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/infarm-raised-500m-and-disappeared#:~:text=Aside%20from%20energy%20costs%2C%20pricing,grown%20indoors%2C%20says%20van%20Rijswick)). Although the technology worked, it was difficult to convince customers to pay significantly more for salads grown indoors, while traditional greenhouses had sunlight for free and lower expenses ([Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/infarm-raised-500m-and-disappeared#:~:text=greenhouses%20have%20%E2%80%9Csunlight%20for%20free%E2%80%9D,at%20Dutch%20cooperative%20bank%20Rabobank)). The Infarm case shows that even with groundbreaking digital innovation, high operating costs and cheaper conventional alternatives can topple even a *unicorn* startup if the economics don't add up.

**Access to capital:** Another barrier is financing and scaling. Europe's venture capital level has traditionally been lower than e.g. the US's – in the last decade approx. 0.2% of GDP against 0.7% in the US ([The Next Web](https://thenextweb.com/news/europe-has-twice-as-many-climate-tech-startups-as-us#:~:text=Europe%20is%20home%20to%20twice,the%20Munich%20Security%20Conference%20today)). Although Europe produces many new green tech companies, they can struggle to raise growth capital. The consequence is that some European startups seek investments outside the EU (e.g. in the US or Asia) to scale, which can mean a loss of jobs and technological lead in Europe ([The Next Web](https://thenextweb.com/news/europe-has-twice-as-many-climate-tech-startups-as-us#:~:text=Europe%20is%20home%20to%20twice,the%20Munich%20Security%20Conference%20today)). The EU's response has partly been to try to strengthen pan-European investment funds and through programs like InvestEU to mobilize private capital for green and digital innovation, but the financing gap is still a challenge on the way to the 2030 goals.

**Regulation and incentives:** Regulation can, as mentioned, be a double-edged sword. For startups, complex approval procedures, different national standards, and general uncertainty about future rules can be a barrier to introducing new products across EU countries. In some sectors, incentives that could accelerate the green transition digitally are still missing. For example, the MedTech industry points out that certain existing rules directly work against digital sustainable solutions – a concrete case is EU requirements for physical instructions for use for medical equipment, where electronic instructions (*e-IFU*) could save paper and waste ([MedTech Europe](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf#:~:text=holistic%20standards%20and%20guidelines%20for,In%20particular)). Likewise, it is mentioned that national health budgets often do not incentivize investment in preventive digital solutions, as the gains appear across silos ([MedTech Europe](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf#:~:text=holistic%20standards%20and%20guidelines%20for,In%20particular)). Lack of *"green"* incentives generally (e.g. low CO2 price, insufficient green public procurement policy, or few tax breaks for green innovation) can mean that sustainable digital products struggle to compete with cheaper, but less sustainable alternatives. Trade organizations therefore recommend removing unnecessary obstacles and creating stronger market signals: e.g. harmonized rules, more value-based public demand, and financial incentives for green digital innovation ([MedTech Europe](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf#:~:text=holistic%20standards%20and%20guidelines%20for,In%20particular)).

Finally, **human resources** are a potential limitation: If the workforce does not have the necessary digital skills, or if talent leaves Europe, it becomes difficult to realize the twin transition. "Brain drain" and competition for specialists (e.g. within AI, data science, cleantech engineers) can hamper Europe's transition if education systems and companies fail to upgrade and retain talent to a sufficient extent.

A related concept is "sustainable digitalization," which refers to the process of digitizing the economy in a long-term, green, and organic way, and which aims to support Europe's twin transition to a green and digital economy. Sustainable digitalization aims to strengthen Europe's digital sovereignty and fundamentally rests on three pillars: sustainable B2B digitalization, greener technologies and a circular economy, and an innovation-promoting political and regulatory framework.

- **Search engines:** Ecosia is a search engine based in Berlin that donates the majority of its profits from advertising to planting trees globally. For every search, revenue is generated that is used to plant trees in over 35 countries in collaboration with local organizations. Ecosia has so far planted over 228 million trees and allocated over 92 million euros to climate action. The company focuses on restoring and protecting biodiversity hotspots by growing over 500 different native species in areas where the need is greatest.
    
- **Data centers:** windCORES is a German company that places energy-intensive data centers directly inside wind turbines. By utilizing the green energy produced by the wind turbines, windCORES reduces CO2 emissions associated with traditional data centers and energy transmission. For every kWh of electricity used, they save over 400g of CO2 compared to traditional energy sources.
    
- **Marketplaces for refurbished electronics:** Back Market is an online marketplace that connects consumers with sellers of refurbished electronic devices. This contributes to sustainability by reducing electronic waste, saving resources, and reducing CO2 emissions by extending the life of electronic devices. Back Market claims to have prevented over 1 million tons of CO2 emissions.
    
- **Micromobility solutions:** TIER Mobility, headquartered in Berlin, offers shared micromobility solutions such as electric scooters, bikes, and mopeds to reduce car use in cities. This provides a cleaner alternative to cars for transport in urban areas and can potentially reduce traffic jams and emissions.
    
- **AI for environmental monitoring:** Several European companies use advanced machine learning to optimize extraction processes, energy consumption, and waste management, leading to increased efficiency and reduced resource consumption.
    
- **Digital twins for sustainability:** Companies like MYX Robotics and MClimate in Central and Eastern Europe offer digital representations of physical systems, so-called digital twins, which enable real-time monitoring, analysis, and optimization of systems across different industries with a view to minimizing environmental impact. This allows for simulating and analyzing environmental consequences before changes are implemented in the real world.
    
- **Software for CO2 accounting:** Companies like Plan A (Bulgaria), EKYO (Romania), and Envirly (Poland) develop SaaS platforms to assess companies' CO2 emissions in supply chains and generate action-oriented data for emission reduction.
    
- **Apps for climate action:** Klima, based in Berlin, is a personal climate action app that helps users calculate and offset their carbon footprint and guides them to reduce their climate impact.
    
- **Sustainable packaging:** PulPac in Sweden supplies production technology for fiber-based packaging as a sustainable and cost-effective alternative to plastic.
    
- **Solutions for e-waste:** Rebel Tech in Germany creates deep-tech solutions for electronics repair. Origina in Ireland focuses on extending the life of software to reduce e-waste.
    
- **Biological plant protection:** AgroSustain in Switzerland offers a comprehensive solution for biological plant protection with the aim of reducing food waste and supporting organic food production through natural alternatives to chemical pesticides. Their products are 100% natural and leave no residues.
    
- **Plastic recycling:** DePoly, also based in Switzerland, has developed an advanced recycling technology that can convert different types of plastic waste, including PET and textiles, into high-quality raw materials. The process takes place at room temperature and normal pressure using safe household chemicals.
    
- **Sustainable materials from coffee grounds:** Ecobean in Poland has developed a technology to convert coffee grounds into sustainable materials and specialty chemicals, including coffee oil, antioxidants, and PLA, reducing waste and carbon footprint.
    
- **Plastic collection from the ocean:** CleanHub, based in Berlin, works to keep plastic out of the ocean by financing and promoting plastic recovery in coastal communities. They use AI-powered technology to track and verify the collection of plastic waste.

The financial sector also sees an increase in digital entrepreneurship promoting sustainability. Platforms like ekko in the UK offer sustainable banking solutions that track carbon footprints and invest in sustainability projects. Impact investment platforms like Carbon Equity in the Netherlands and Clim8 in the UK give investors access to climate-focused investment funds. TreeCard in the UK is a payment card that plants trees for every purchase, and Tomorrow in Germany offers sustainable and transparent mobile banking services that finance climate initiatives.

Within the MedTech sector, digital technology contributes to sustainability by improving efficiency and enabling remote care. eHealth solutions promote efficiency in the healthcare system and allow patients to play a more active role in managing their health. AI is used for optimized diagnosis and remote monitoring of patients. Initiatives like SUSA (Sustainable Healthcare with Digital Health Data Competence) aim to develop digital skills for sustainable health solutions.

The future of the twin transition in the EU holds significant opportunities for the tech sector. Increased investments in green and digital technology driven by EU policies and goals are expected. The demand for AI, IoT, big data, and cloud computing solutions for sustainability applications is expected to rise. New markets will likely emerge in areas such as sustainable digital infrastructure, carbon capture technologies, and circular economy solutions. Europe has the potential to become a leader in the development and implementation of green technology solutions. Job creation is also expected in the green and digital sectors. The combination of ambitious EU environmental goals and the transformative power of digital technology creates a strong driver for innovation. Entrepreneurs who can anticipate future needs and develop scalable solutions in areas such as clean energy, sustainable agriculture, and smart cities will likely find significant opportunities.

However, there are also potential pitfalls. There is a risk that the environmental gains of digital technology may be outweighed by their own environmental impact, such as energy consumption and e-waste. It is also a challenge to ensure that digitalization does not reinforce unsustainable consumption and growth models. Tensions may arise between the "logic of limits" in the green transition and the "logic of unlimited possibilities" in the digital transition. There is also a risk of overlooking critical environmental challenges that cannot easily be solved with digital technology. It is necessary to address the lack of digital skills to fully realize the potential of the twin transition. Challenges related to data protection, cybersecurity, and digital inequality must also be handled. There is potential for increased inequalities and polarization of the labor market due to unequal access to digital technology and skills. Finally, there is a risk of "green extractivism" in connection with the materials needed for green technologies. To realize the full potential of the twin transition, careful consideration of potential trade-offs and unintended consequences is required, ensuring a holistic approach that prioritizes both environmental sustainability and social justice. The twin transition is not without challenges. Policymakers and entrepreneurs must be aware of the potential for digital technology to exacerbate existing environmental problems or create new ones. A focus on resource efficiency, circularity, and ethical considerations is crucial to ensure that the transition is truly sustainable.

A policy brief from the European Commission's Directorate-General for Research and Innovation emphasizes the importance of integrating foresight, flexibility, and ethics into the EU's regulatory frameworks to navigate both risks and opportunities of new technologies. Reports highlight the significant energy consumption of digital technology and the need for a more sustainable digital sector. The 2022 Strategic Foresight Report and a warning from the Joint Research Centre (JRC) point to the tensions between the two transitions. Ongoing research and analysis are essential to understand the evolving dynamics of the twin transition and to inform effective policy and business strategies. The field of digital entrepreneurship for sustainability is constantly evolving. It is important for both policymakers and entrepreneurs to stay informed about the latest research, trends, and policy developments to make informed decisions and navigate the opportunities and challenges that lie ahead.
