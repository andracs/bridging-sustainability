# 9. Governing sustainability in digital projects

In the previous chapter we explored movements that question the growth narrative of IT. In this chapter we return to the world most readers work in: companies, organizations and projects that must earn their keep. How do you *govern* digital work so that sustainability is not a poster on the wall but a property of the decisions being made? And if you want to build something yourself - a product, a venture, a business - where is the opportunity? We look at governance, business models, entrepreneurship and leadership, and at European companies trying to make sustainability and digitalization pull in the same direction. Some of them are thriving. Some of them are not. Both kinds have something to teach you.

## Why governance matters in digital sustainability

Every chapter so far has ended with things *you* can do: choose efficient languages, repairable hardware, loyal patterns. But individual good intentions evaporate in organizations that measure and reward something else. Governance is the connective tissue between values and behavior: the structures, incentives, policies and decision rights that determine what actually gets built.

The starting question of any digital governance discussion should be disarmingly simple: *What is information technology good for?* If the honest answer for a given project is "engagement", "growth" or "because our competitors have it", the sustainability analysis from chapters 2-4 has not yet been done. Governance for sustainability begins by moving the question from compliance ("what must we document?") to strategy ("what long-term value do we want to create, and for whom?").

This mirrors a broader shift in how companies are evaluated. As we saw in chapter 1, the triple bottom line and ESG frameworks extend the definition of performance to people and planet; as we saw in chapter 7, the CSRD makes parts of that reporting mandatory for the largest EU companies. Governance is how an organization turns those external expectations into internal practice.

## From profits to purpose

Is sustainability actually good for business? The honest answer deserves more respect than the usual slogans. Many unsustainable companies have grown large and profitable, so sustainability has clearly never been a prerequisite for success. At the same time, a growing body of research suggests that sustainable companies enjoy real advantages: brand reputation, customer loyalty, operational efficiency, better long-term risk management.

Sustainability is neither a guaranteed profit machine nor a luxury. It is a bet that the future will price in externalities the present still ignores - through regulation, customer expectations, talent preferences and resource scarcity. Governing for sustainability means placing that bet deliberately rather than by accident.

### Governance structures for sustainability

What does such governance look like in practice? The building blocks are familiar from chapters 1 and 6, and the task is to assemble them:

- **Accountability**: someone - a person, a team, ideally with board-level sponsorship - owns the sustainability agenda for digital activities, with a mandate and a budget.
- **Measurement**: selected indicators (for example from the SustainableIT standards in chapter 6, or the SCI specification in chapter 5) are tracked with the same seriousness as financial KPIs.
- **Decision gates**: sustainability criteria appear where decisions are actually made - in procurement policies, architecture reviews, definition-of-done checklists and supplier contracts - not only in the annual report.
- **Incentives**: what gets rewarded gets repeated. If teams are bonused on engagement metrics alone, chapter 7's attention economy will win every argument.

None of this is exotic. It is the ordinary machinery of corporate governance pointed at a new target, and the EU's twin transition agenda (chapter 1) increasingly assumes exactly this: that digital and green objectives are governed together rather than in separate silos.

## By IT and of IT

A useful distinction for any governance discussion is between sustainability *by* IT and sustainability *of* IT. Sustainability *by* IT uses digital tools to make something else more sustainable: optimizing a heating grid, reducing food waste, enabling telemedicine. Sustainability *of* IT makes the digital itself more sustainable: efficient software, circular hardware, green data centers - the subjects of chapters 3-6.

The distinction matters because organizations love to talk about the first while ignoring the second. A company can build a brilliant energy-optimization platform (by IT) on wasteful infrastructure with exploitative supply chains (of IT). Mature governance holds both to account - and remembers the rebound effect: gains *by* IT must be significantly larger than the footprint *of* the IT that delivers them, as we saw with the district heating example in chapter 1.

## The power of storytelling

Numbers govern budgets, but stories govern attention. Throughout this book we have seen companies - Fairphone, Framework, Velux - use *sustainability stories* to align employees, customers and investors around a direction. Stories are how strategy travels through an organization: "the sustainability journey" is retold in onboarding, sales meetings and investor decks until it becomes culture.

Storytelling is also where governance meets the greenwashing risks of chapter 1. A story that runs ahead of the data is a liability - legally, after the EmpCo directive, and reputationally. The governance task is to keep story and substance coupled: every claim in the narrative should trace back to a measured indicator. Or as one might put it: stories are important, but value propositions are importanter.

### Case story: A radiator with a battery inside

Across Europe, millions of homes rely on electric heaters that burden power grids at peak times and strain household budgets. Meanwhile, the shift to renewables demands a grid that can flex around when the sun shines and the wind blows. French entrepreneur Raphaël Meyer saw this double inefficiency not as a barrier but as a business: in 2016 he founded Lancey Energy Storage in Grenoble.

Meyer had pivoted from photovoltaics research towards energy storage, initially exploring sodium-based batteries - a technology that turned out not to be mature enough at the time. His core insight survived the pivot: real efficiency often lies in turning unavoidable energy losses into useful outputs. Just as waste heat from data centers can warm nearby housing, the heat generated when charging a battery can warm a room. So why not put the battery *inside* the radiator?

Lancey's smart radiator charges when electricity is cheap and heats when it is expensive. It stores off-peak or surplus solar power, supports grid stability, cuts emissions from peaking power plants and lowers heating bills. Meyer's wider vision reaches further: buildings with battery storage as standard as any other appliance, local energy management that frees buildings from the grid's timing, heating-and-storage sold "as a service" so users face no upfront cost - even a society that runs its aluminum, steel and concrete production in seasons of renewable abundance, storing value in products rather than only in batteries.

And yet. Despite an award-winning product, patents and public funding, the numbers would not close. Lancey entered receivership in mid-2024, went into liquidation that autumn, and was acquired by the French energy-renovation group Votre Artisan Vert, under whose ownership the team is now developing a new product range ([Présences Grenoble 2025](https://www.presences-grenoble.fr/actualites-industrie-grenoble/rachetee-par-votre-artisan-vert-lancey-energy-storage-se-relance.htm)).

As Meyer himself emphasizes: ideas alone are not enough to make a business. Operational details make the difference. Keep that sentence in mind for the rest of this chapter - it is the most valuable thing a sustainable entrepreneur can learn secondhand rather than firsthand.

## Sustainable digital entrepreneurship

**Digital entrepreneurship** is the creation of new or innovative internet-based companies, products or services - whether as a startup or as digital transformation inside an established organization. What makes it interesting for our purposes is its scalability: software that solves a sustainability problem once can solve it a million times at marginal cost. At the intersection with sustainable development we find *digital sustainable entrepreneurship*: business models that use digital technologies to create environmental and social value on purpose, not as a byproduct.

### Business model innovation

A practical starting point is the Business Model Canvas: the familiar one-page map of customer segments, value propositions, channels, revenue streams, key resources, activities, partners and costs. For a sustainable digital venture, two extensions matter.

First, the *value proposition* must be honest about who pays and who benefits - including nature and future generations as silent stakeholders, in the spirit of chapter 4's non-human personas.

Second, the cost and revenue logic must survive the "green premium" test: will customers pay extra for the sustainability, or must the model win on ordinary merits - savings, convenience, quality - with sustainability as the bonus? As the Lancey case shows, and the Infarm case below will confirm, models that *require* customers to pay for virtue are fragile. Models where sustainability and self-interest align are robust.

### Strategies for acquiring funding

Europe has seen a wave of tech startups combining digital innovation with green causes. In fact, Europe is home to roughly twice as many *climate tech* startups as the US - about 30,000 against 14,300 - despite chronically thinner venture funding ([World Fund](https://www.worldfund.vc/knowledge/white-paper-climate-tech-for-european-resilience)). Funding routes range from classic venture capital, through dedicated ESG and impact funds, to bootstrapping - growing on revenue without external investors. Each carries a governance consequence: venture capital typically demands growth rates that can conflict with sufficiency thinking, impact funds demand measurable outcomes, and bootstrapping preserves autonomy at the price of speed. Choosing a funding model is choosing a set of masters; a sustainable venture should choose masters whose incentives it can live with.

### Value chains and ecosystems

No digital product exists alone. Chapter 1 introduced digital ecosystems, and chapter 3 mapped the physical supply chains behind hardware; the entrepreneurial task is to know your own position in these chains. A typical industrial-digital value chain runs from a *device layer* (sensors on equipment), through a *connectivity layer* (networks transmitting data) and a *platform layer* (cloud services storing and organizing it), to an *analytics layer* (models detecting patterns) and an *application layer* (dashboards and feedback to operators). Each layer has its own sustainability profile - and its own dependency risks, which is where the digital sovereignty perspective from chapter 8 re-enters: open standards, open source and lifecycle assessment tools such as openLCA (chapter 5) keep your options open. Stakeholder mapping completes the picture: who, human and non-human, is affected at each layer?

## Sustainability by IT: cases from the European twin transition

Digital technologies such as AI, IoT and data analytics can help other industries cut their emissions; analyses suggest that digitalization could enable reductions of up to 20% of global emissions by 2030 ([DigitalEurope](https://www.digitaleurope.org/resources/digital-action-climate-action-8-ideas-to-accelerate-the-twin-transition/)). Treat that figure with chapter 2's critical toolkit - reported savings are claims to be examined, not verdicts - but even a fraction of it makes tech entrepreneurs key players in the green transition. Consider what European companies are already doing.

In the energy sector, Germany's **Next Kraftwerke** operates one of Europe's largest *virtual power plants*: a digital platform aggregating thousands of decentralized wind turbines, solar parks, biogas plants and batteries, trading and balancing them as if they were a single power station ([Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/start-next-kraftwerkes-renewable-virtual-power-plant-stabilises-grid)). It is a textbook example of software substituting for fossil infrastructure: the "plant" is code.

In food, Denmark's **Too Good To Go** connects consumers with restaurants, bakeries and supermarkets holding surplus food. Within three and a half years of its 2016 launch it had saved 29 million meals and avoided more than 72,000 tons of CO2 - equivalent to taking 15,000 cars off the road for a year - and the model has since scaled internationally ([Zero Waste Europe](https://zerowasteeurope.eu/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/zero_waste_europe_CS7_CP_TooGoodToGo_en.pdf)). The genius is that the sustainability and the bargain are the same transaction: nobody pays a green premium, everybody wins.

In finance, Sweden's **Doconomy** converts payment transactions into CO2 estimates, giving consumers and banks insight into the climate impact of consumption, and has attracted substantial investment to scale its tools ([ESG Today](https://www.esgtoday.com/climate-impact-fintech-doconomy-raises-37-million-to-help-consumers-make-sustainable-choices/)). Its DO card - a credit card with a CO2 ceiling that stops purchases when the user's climate budget is spent - remains a thought-provoking piece of loyal-pattern design ([Thales Group](https://www.thalesgroup.com/en/markets/digital-identity-and-security/banking-payment/inspired/doconomy)). Among established players, the Dutch **Triodos Bank** has financed only projects with positive environmental or social impact since its founding, and publishes every loan for full transparency.

In health, telemedicine shows what avoided travel adds up to: a systematic review of 48 studies found that roughly 68 million telemedicine consultations saved approximately 691,000 tons of CO2 and 3.3 billion kilometers of travel ([European Urology Focus 2023](https://www.eu-focus.europeanurology.com/article/S2405-4569(23)00272-9/abstract)). Platforms such as Sweden's Kry/Livi have delivered millions of online consultations, and MedTech Europe argues that climate and health policy should be designed together, with digitally supported, resource-efficient care pathways ([MedTech Europe 2025](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf)).

And the list keeps growing: search engines that fund reforestation (Ecosia, Germany), data centers placed inside wind turbines (windCORES, Germany), marketplaces for refurbished electronics (Back Market, France), CO2-accounting SaaS platforms, plastic-to-raw-material recycling (DePoly, Switzerland), AI-verified ocean plastic recovery (CleanHub, Germany). The pattern across all of them: digital tools applied to measurement, matching and optimization problems that markets alone were not solving. That pattern is repeatable - and there are far more such problems than there are companies working on them. One of them could be yours.

## Leadership in sustainable digital projects

Governance provides structure; leadership provides direction. A few principles recur among the leaders of the companies in this chapter and this book.

They focus on core strengths: Fairphone did not try to fix everything at once, but chose a few material flows and worked them relentlessly. They lead regeneratively, applying chapter 4's design principle to organizations - leaving the teams, communities and ecosystems they touch in better condition than they found them, which includes the work-life sustainability from chapter 1. They navigate standards deliberately, using the certifications and frameworks from earlier chapters - B Corp, EcoVadis, SustainableIT, SCI, CSRD - as tools that translate ambition into commitments outliving individual enthusiasm.

And they mind the human factor, because transitions fail on culture, not spreadsheets. The IT-Branchen recommendations in chapter 6 put it plainly: involve key employees from all areas, and invest in green skills.

## Barriers and pitfalls

The potential is great; so are the well-known barriers. Take them seriously - they are where good intentions go to die.

The first is brutally simple: green business models must be able to compete economically. The Berlin startup **Infarm** specialized in digitally controlled vertical farming, grew rapidly and raised almost $500 million in capital - and had to declare insolvency in 2023, because the model could not sustain the high energy costs and green-premium prices that indoor plant production entailed. Traditional greenhouses had sunlight for free ([Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/infarm-raised-500m-and-disappeared)). Even groundbreaking digital innovation cannot outrun an economic model that does not add up.

Access to capital is the second barrier. Europe's venture capital level has traditionally been far lower than the US's - roughly 0.2% of GDP against 0.7% over the last decade ([The Next Web](https://thenextweb.com/news/europe-has-twice-as-many-climate-tech-startups-as-us)). Europe produces many green tech companies but struggles to fund their scaling, pushing some to seek capital - and eventually jobs and IP - outside the EU. Programs like InvestEU attempt to close the gap, but the financing shortfall remains a real constraint.

Regulation cuts both ways. Complex approval procedures and diverging national rules burden startups, and in some sectors existing rules actively work against sustainable digital solutions - MedTech Europe points, for example, to EU requirements for paper instructions where electronic ones would save material, and to health budgets that do not reward preventive digital care because the gains land in other silos ([MedTech Europe 2025](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf)). Weak market signals - a low carbon price, timid green public procurement - let cheaper, less sustainable alternatives win.

People are a barrier too. Europe is estimated to need 20 million ICT specialists by 2030 to drive the digital transition ([BFNA](https://www.bfna.org/politics-society/the-future-of-work-in-the-twin-transition-to-green-and-digital-3xn0a3queo/)); if education systems and companies fail to develop and retain talent in software, data and green technology, the twin transition stalls on human resources.

And finally, recall chapter 8: there is an inherent tension between the "logic of limits" in the green transition and the "logic of unlimited possibilities" in the digital one. Digital solutions can reinforce unsustainable consumption, their own footprint can outweigh their gains, and the materials behind green technology carry extractive risks of their own. A truly sustainable twin transition demands resource efficiency, circularity and ethical vigilance - not just enthusiasm.

## Future prospects for the digital and green transition

The EU's vision for 2030 contains ambitious goals for both transformations. Under *Europe's Digital Decade*, targets include that 75% of EU companies should use cloud, AI or big data, and that 10,000 climate-neutral, highly secure edge data centers should be established across Europe ([European Commission](https://commission.europa.eu/strategy-and-policy/priorities-2019-2024/europe-fit-digital-age/europes-digital-decade-digital-targets-2030_en)). These digital goals are designed to go hand in hand with the Green Deal objectives of 55% CO2 reduction by 2030 and climate neutrality by 2050, and the EU's recovery fund required member states to allocate at least 37% of budgets to green and 20% to digital initiatives ([European Commission](https://commission.europa.eu/business-economy-euro/economic-recovery/recovery-and-resilience-facility_en)). Estimates put the required investment at up to 745 billion euros towards 2030 ([DigitalEurope](https://www.digitaleurope.org/news/joint-statement-a-call-to-action-for-a-digital-single-market-by-2030/)).

For you, the signal is clear: capital, regulation and demand are converging on the intersection of digital and green. New ventures in green AI, climate finance, energy storage, smart cities and circular economy will find both opportunity and scrutiny there - scrutiny being the operative word, as the EmpCo rules from chapter 1 raise the price of unsubstantiated claims. The winners will be the ones whose stories and spreadsheets tell the same truth.

## Bringing it together: Key insights from this chapter

Sustainability in digital projects does not happen by itself; it requires intentional governance that aligns people, policies and technologies. The journey runs from compliance to strategy: from documenting what regulators demand to deciding what long-term value the organization exists to create. Stories shape culture, but strategy - anchored in measured indicators and honest value propositions - sustains outcomes.

The distinction between sustainability *by* IT and *of* IT keeps organizations honest: celebrate the emissions your platform saves, but account for the footprint it costs. The European cases show that the twin transition is a genuine business landscape, and the Lancey and Infarm stories show it is an unforgiving one: resilience and operational discipline decide who survives to deliver impact.

Leadership sets direction; governance builds structure; entrepreneurship delivers impact. Whichever of the three is yours to give, the preceding chapters have supplied the tools - and the next, final chapter looks at where the road leads.
