## 📗 Get the book

If you write code, design interfaces, run infrastructure, or manage digital projects, your daily decisions carry an environmental and social footprint — and more leverage over it than you might think. 

This book turns that leverage into practice. 🌱 It is not a manifesto and not a research paper: it is a practitioner's guide that walks you from the fundamental concepts of sustainability, through systems thinking, hardware, software, design, well-being, degrowth, and digital business, to concrete checklists you can apply in your next project. 

Every factual claim is checked against the current state of the art, every chapter ends with reflection questions, and the whole book is written to one purpose: helping you practice your digital profession more sustainably, starting now.

## 🗺️ How the book is built

This repository holds the manuscript - and also out todo lists. If you rather not read the markdown files, please consider buying a copy. Also, you are very welcone to contribute, if you feel like helping with the todo-lists or improving the book other ways.

Below is an overview of how each chapter is structured and the line of thought running through it.

### 🌍 Chapter 1 — Setting the stage

One continuous zooming movement from the planet to the reader. It opens with the scientific case for urgency (the Anthropocene, mass extinction, the Great Acceleration, planetary boundaries, climate change) and lands the point that carries the book: IT professionals are both villains and heroes. It then equips the reader step by step — the vocabulary of sustainability, the physics of energy and materials ♻️, the organizational level (ESG, circular economy, the 7 R's), and the societal and individual level (SDGs, regulation, well-being) — before converging on the digital field through the Karlskrona Manifesto, the twin transition, and digitainability.

### 🧭 Chapter 2 — Critical thinking and systems theory

A toolbox chapter: it builds the analytical lens the rest of the book looks through. It opens with the Karlskrona premise — *"sustainability is systemic"* — made vivid by the video-streaming example, then defines the anatomy of a system, its types (emergent, designed, digital twins), and introduces critical thinking as the corrective filter against greenwashed schemes and the rebound effect. The theory is cashed out in applied models — the lake vs. data center comparison and an e-commerce system analyzed through all five Karlskrona dimensions — before network theory extends the toolbox to connections and cascade effects.

### ⚡ Chapter 3 — The impact of information technology

A parable followed by a dissection. The chapter opens with the story of how Earth's orbit went from pristine to hazardous in a few decades 🛰️ — a cautionary tale about technological growth without foresight — then dissects IT into its three layers, hardware, network, and software, and examines the sustainability of each: supply chains and e-waste, data center energy, AI's footprint, technical debt, and user well-being. The accumulated problems resolve into a strategy pair: reactive (mitigate what exists) and proactive (design sustainability in from the start).

### 🎨 Chapter 4 — Digital design with sustainability

A descent from philosophy to pixel. Because the design phase sets a product's entire lifecycle, the chapter starts where the leverage is greatest: three increasingly ambitious design philosophies (Design for the New World, Life-centered design, regenerative design 🌿), then moves through UX (sustainable user journeys, designing with data) down to the interface itself — dark vs. loyal patterns, web emissions, the W3C Web Sustainability Guidelines, and optimization tools — closing with nudging as a way to guide users toward sustainable behavior.

### 💻 Chapter 5 — Sustainability in software development

The software development life cycle (SDLC) is the chapter's spine, and sustainability is woven into every phase of it. The hardest problem comes first — measuring software's energy use (lab vs. production, LCA, Software Carbon Intensity) — followed by ecolabels like the Blue Angel, and then the hands-on core: green coding, programming-language energy profiles, green prompting for AI 🤖, algorithmic complexity, the GREENER principles for research software, and finally architecture and DevOps, where efficiency becomes an operational habit.

### 🔧 Chapter 6 — (Not quite) sustainable hardware and IT operations

The parenthesis in the title is the thesis. Three honest cases — the fair computer mouse, Framework, and Fairphone — show that even the most determined companies can only build hardware that is *less unsustainable*, not truly green. From that honest verdict the chapter opens two horizons: a thought experiment about genuinely sustainable computers, and the lo-fi/low-carbon/collapse-informatics traditions — before returning to what organizations can do today: greener IT operations, IT-Branchen's 9 recommendations, and the SustainableIT standards. ♻️

### 💚 Chapter 7 — Wellness, well-being and digitalization

The chapter where "soft values" become hard requirements. It opens with the problem — attention theft, documented harms, unhappiness in a technological world — then follows two responses: the political one (regulation and CSRD's documentation requirements for social sustainability) and the research one (digital well-being tools at the individual and organizational level). AI gets its own treatment as the fastest-moving frontier — companions, content, and care — before the chapter lands in practice: a playbook for socially sustainable UX and action from NGOs.

### 🌱 Chapter 8 — Digital degrowth

The book's counter-narrative chapter. It questions the growth story that frames most visions of IT's future — Moore's law as destiny — using Eroom's law as the opening counterexample, then tours the research communities (LOCOS, Computing within Limits) and radical practices (permacomputing, a solar-powered website ☀️, convivial and junkyard computing) that design within real-world limits. European digital sovereignty and open-source alternatives ground the ideas geopolitically, before the chapter confronts the ultimate growth narrative of our time: the AI boom and its scaling laws.

### 🏢 Chapter 9 — Governing sustainability in digital projects

Back to the world most readers work in: organizations that must earn their keep. The premise is that individual good intentions evaporate without structures, so the chapter builds them: governance (accountability, measurement, incentives), the *by IT / of IT* distinction, storytelling as the vehicle that carries strategy through a culture, and sustainable digital entrepreneurship — business model innovation, funding routes, and value chains — illustrated with European twin-transition cases, leadership principles, and honest pitfalls like the Infarm collapse.
