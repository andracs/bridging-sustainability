# List of Academic Discrepancies

| Chapter/Section | Type of Error | The Issue | Suggestion |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| Chapter 3, line 194 | Unit Error / Factual Inconsistency | "Raghavendra Selvan (DTU) estimated that a single ChatGPT prompt uses approximately 0.1938 kWh, comparable to charging a smartphone about 40 times." This value appears unrealistically high. A typical smartphone battery is 10-15 Wh, so 40 charges would be 0.4-0.6 kWh. More critically, 0.1938 kWh (194 Wh) per single prompt seems implausible for inference (this would be more typical of training energy). The citation points to a 2024 article, but the value needs verification against current research. | Verify the actual energy consumption figure. If this refers to training rather than inference, clarify. If it's inference, the value may be off by orders of magnitude (typical inference is measured in Wh, not kWh per prompt). |
| Chapter 8, lines 25-27 | Temporal Discrepancy / Contradiction | Moore's Law is presented as still active: "computing power doubles every 1½ years" and "leads many to assume that the current trend of ever-increasing production and consumption will continue." However, many experts argue Moore's Law has slowed significantly or effectively ended since ~2015-2020. The chapter doesn't acknowledge this debate, which contradicts the degrowth theme that questions unlimited growth assumptions. | Add a note that Moore's Law has slowed significantly since ~2015, with transistor density improvements now taking 2-3 years rather than 18 months. Acknowledge the debate about whether it has effectively ended. |
| Chapter 8, line 40 | Unsupported Claim | "According to them, there is a risk that by 2040, computers alone will generate more than half of the emissions acceptable to keep global warming below 1.5°C." This is a very specific and alarming claim, but no citation is provided for the LOCOS research group's methodology or data source. | Add a specific citation to the research paper or report that contains this calculation, including methodology. |
| Chapter 5, line 236 | Unsupported Claim | "Research shows that huge amounts of data are created that are not used later and just take up space in the computers." This is a broad claim without a specific citation. | Provide a citation to specific research studies that quantify this claim (e.g., percentage of unused data, specific studies). |
| Chapter 6, line 134 | Temporal Discrepancy | "It is estimated to be about twice as high as it is now, and three to four times higher by 2040." The phrase "as it is now" is ambiguous - "now" relative to when? The book states it reflects "the situation as of 2025" (Introduction), but this estimate may be based on older data. | Clarify the baseline year for "now" and provide the source and date of the estimate. If the estimate is from before 2024, note this temporal limitation. |
| Chapter 1, line 281 | Scope Confusion (Minor) | The definition of Scope 1, 2, and 3 is generally correct, but the text says "three types of CO2 emissions" when the GHG Protocol actually covers all greenhouse gases (CO2, CH4, N2O, etc.), not just CO2. While CO2 is the most common, the terminology should be more precise. | Change "CO2 emissions" to "greenhouse gas emissions" or "GHG emissions" to align with the GHG Protocol's actual scope. |
| Chapter 8, line 92 | Unit Error | "It consumes a maximum of 2.5 Wh. The system is powered by an off-grid solar system in Barcelona and the solar array consists of a 30W solar panel and a 168 Wh lead-acid battery." The text correctly uses Wh for energy (battery capacity) and W for power (solar panel), but the phrase "consumes a maximum of 2.5 Wh" is ambiguous - is this per hour, per day, or total? | Clarify whether 2.5 Wh is per hour (2.5 W power consumption) or total energy consumption over a specific time period. |
| Chapter 5, line 327 | Temporal Discrepancy | "According to a major new IEA report, global electricity demand from data centers is projected to more than double by 2030." The text says "major new IEA report" but doesn't specify the publication date. Given the book is "as of 2025," this may be outdated. | Provide the specific IEA report title, publication date, and report number. If it's from 2023 or earlier, note that projections may have been updated. |
| Chapter 9, line 119 | Unsupported Claim | "Digital technologies such as AI, IoT, and data analytics can help other industries reduce their CO2 emissions; analyses suggest that digitalization can enable a reduction of up to 20% of global emissions by 2030." The phrase "analyses suggest" is vague. While a citation to "Digital Europe" is provided, the specific methodology or report should be clearer. | Provide more specific citation details (report title, date, methodology) for the "20% reduction" claim, or note the uncertainty/range of estimates in the literature. |
| Chapter 3, line 85 | Temporal Discrepancy | "Globally, a mere 17.4% of e-waste is estimated to be collected and appropriately recycled." The citation is to United Nations University 2024, but e-waste statistics are typically published with a 1-2 year lag. The "17.4%" figure may be from 2022 or 2023 data, not 2024. | Verify the actual data year in the UNU source. If it's 2022 or 2023 data, note this explicitly (e.g., "17.4% as of 2022" or "according to 2023 data"). |
| Chapter 1, line 298 | Unsupported Claim (Minor) | "According to a study by behavioral economist Peter Andre and his colleagues, '69 percent of the world's population is willing to sacrifice one percent of their income for climate action'... and Myanmar tops the list with 92.80%." While cited, the specific percentage (92.80%) with two decimal places suggests precision that may not be meaningful for survey data. | The citation is provided, but consider whether such precise percentages (92.80%) are statistically meaningful or should be rounded (e.g., "approximately 93%"). |
| Chapter 8, line 31 | Temporal Discrepancy | "In this sense, one could say that blockchain technology is currently in the trough of disillusionment." The word "currently" is relative to the writing date. If written in 2024-2025, blockchain may have moved past this stage (e.g., Bitcoin ETF approvals, institutional adoption). | Either remove "currently" or specify the assessment date. Note that technology positions on hype cycles are subjective and time-dependent. |
| Chapter 5, line 218 | Contradiction | The text states "Python and Perl, which in some studies are 70-80 times more energy hungry than C" but then immediately notes "Python is not an overall-bad-choice energy-wise, because there are also studies showing that Python is more efficient than C# and Java in some cases." This creates confusion - if Python is 70-80x worse than C but sometimes better than C#/Java, what is the relationship between C and C#/Java? | Clarify the energy efficiency hierarchy. If C is the baseline, then C#/Java are X times worse than C, and Python is 70-80x worse than C, then Python should be worse than C#/Java. The contradiction suggests different benchmarks or contexts. |
| Chapter 4, line 196 | Temporal Discrepancy | "Version 1.0 of this document is still only a draft in 2025" - This is a temporal marker that will become outdated. If the book is published in 2025 or later, this should be updated or made more general. | Either update the status if WSG 1.0 has been finalized, or use more general language like "as of [publication date], version 1.0 was still in draft." |
| Chapter 9, line 163 | Temporal Discrepancy | Multiple references to "2030 targets" and "by 2030" without specifying the baseline year for projections. The EU's Digital Decade targets were set in 2021, so "by 2030" means 9 years from 2021, but if read in 2025, it's only 5 years away. | Clarify that these are EU targets set in 2021 for achievement by 2030, and note the remaining timeline from the book's publication date. |
