# 9. Governing sustainability in digital projects

In the previous chapter we explored movements that question the growth narrative of IT. In this chapter we return to the world most readers work in: companies, organizations and projects that must earn their keep. How do you *govern* digital work so that sustainability is not a poster on the wall but a property of the decisions being made? We look at governance, business models, entrepreneurship and leadership - and at European companies that are trying to make sustainability and digitalization pull in the same direction.

## Why governance matters in digital sustainability

Every chapter so far has ended with things *you* can do: choose efficient languages, repairable hardware, loyal patterns. But individual good intentions evaporate in organizations that measure and reward something else. Governance is the connective tissue between values and behavior: the structures, incentives, policies and decision rights that determine what actually gets built.

The starting question of any digital governance discussion should be disarmingly simple: *What is information technology good for?* If the honest answer for a given project is "engagement", "growth" or "because our competitors have it", the sustainability analysis from chapters 2-4 has not yet been done. Governance for sustainability begins by moving the question from compliance ("what must we document?") to strategy ("what long-term value do we want to create, and for whom?").

This mirrors a broader shift in how companies are evaluated: from short-term profits to long-term value creation. As we saw in chapter 1, the triple bottom line and ESG frameworks extend the definition of performance to people and planet; as we saw in chapter 7, the CSRD makes parts of that reporting mandatory for the largest EU companies. Governance is how an organization turns those external expectations into internal practice.

## From profits to purpose

### Sustainable profits?

Sustainability is the idea of conducting business in a way that can be maintained over the long term, without depleting natural resources or harming the environment. We might theorize that a sustainable company could be more resilient and robust in the long run compared to less sustainable businesses. But is that actually the case? Is sustainability truly good for business over time?

On one hand, we see that many unsustainable companies have grown large and profitable, so historically, sustainability has not been a prerequisite for success. On the other hand, there is growing research suggesting that sustainable companies may enjoy certain competitive advantages, such as improved brand reputation, customer loyalty, operational efficiency, and long-term risk management. The honest answer is that sustainability is neither a guaranteed profit machine nor a luxury: it is a bet that the future will price in externalities that the present still ignores - through regulation, customer expectations, talent preferences and resource scarcity. Governing for sustainability means placing that bet deliberately rather than by accident.

### Governance structures for sustainability

What does such governance look like in practice? The building blocks are familiar from chapters 1 and 6, and the task is to assemble them:

- **Accountability**: someone - a person, a team, ideally with board-level sponsorship - owns the sustainability agenda for digital activities, with a mandate and a budget.
- **Measurement**: selected indicators (for example from the SustainableIT standards in chapter 6, or the SCI specification in chapter 5) are tracked with the same seriousness as financial KPIs.
- **Decision gates**: sustainability criteria appear where decisions are actually made - in procurement policies, architecture reviews, definition-of-done checklists and supplier contracts - not only in the annual report.
- **Incentives**: what gets rewarded gets repeated. If teams are bonused on engagement metrics alone, chapter 7's attention economy will win every argument.

None of this is exotic; it is the ordinary machinery of corporate governance pointed at a new target. The EU's twin transition agenda (chapter 1) increasingly assumes exactly this: that digital and green objectives are governed together rather than in separate silos.

## By IT and of IT

A useful distinction for any governance discussion is between sustainability *by* IT and sustainability *of* IT. Sustainability *by* IT uses digital tools to make something else more sustainable: optimizing a heating grid, reducing food waste, enabling telemedicine. Sustainability *of* IT makes the digital itself more sustainable: efficient software, circular hardware, green data centers - the subjects of chapters 3-6.

The distinction matters because organizations love to talk about the first while ignoring the second. A company can build a brilliant energy-optimization platform (by IT) on wasteful infrastructure with exploitative supply chains (of IT). Mature governance holds both to account - and remembers the rebound effect: gains *by* IT must be significantly larger than the footprint *of* the IT that delivers them, as we saw with the district heating example in chapter 1.

## The power of storytelling

Numbers govern budgets, but stories govern attention. Throughout this book we have seen companies - Fairphone, Framework, Velux - use *sustainability stories* to align employees, customers and investors around a direction. Stories are how strategy travels through an organization: "the sustainability journey" is retold in onboarding, sales meetings and investor decks until it becomes culture.

Storytelling is also where governance meets the greenwashing risks of chapter 1. A story that runs ahead of the data is a liability - legally, after the EmpCo directive, and reputationally. The governance task is to keep story and substance coupled: every claim in the narrative should trace back to a measured indicator. Or as one might put it: stories are important, but value propositions are importanter.

### Case story: Combining heating and electrical energy storage in one solution

Across Europe, countless homes rely on electric heaters which burden power grids during peak times and add high costs to household budgets. Simultaneously, the shift towards renewable energy sources like solar power demands a more flexible, responsive electricity grid. French entrepreneur Raphaël Meyer saw this inefficiency not as a barrier, but as the inspiration to start Lancey Energy Storage and develop a smarter heating system in line with the European energy transition.

Raphaël Meyer pivoted from his research in photovoltaics in the 2010s towards energy storage, initially exploring sodium as a basis for batteries, which proved to be an insufficiently mature battery technology at the time.

Meyer's core insight was that true energy efficiency often lies in turning unavoidable energy losses into useful outputs. Just as the heat from data center cooling can be used for heating nearby housing, the heat generated when charging batteries can be used for heating in a radiator. This led to the idea of integrating a battery into a radiator, using the battery's waste heat for room heating, thus boosting overall system efficiency.

Lancey developed a smart radiator with an integrated lithium-ion battery, which can be charged while electricity is cheap and can provide heat while electricity is expensive. This optimizes heating, stores cheap off-peak or surplus solar energy for high self-consumption, supports grid stability, reduces CO2 emissions from peak power plants, and lowers heating bills. Furthermore, it facilitates solar power use and self-consumption.

Despite developing an innovative, award-winning product and securing funding, Lancey faced business challenges and had to change track in 2025. However, the core vision of turning energy inefficiencies into business opportunities persists, highlighting the importance of entrepreneurial resilience required in sustainability-focused ventures.

Meyer is optimistic about the future of sustainable energy, and has many good ideas for the energy transition. His entrepreneurial vision for a future with renewable energy contains, among others, the following ideas:

1. "A society focused on seasonal production rather than just seasonal storage". He envisions doubling industrial capacity for materials like aluminum, steel, and concrete, running these facilities primarily during periods with plenty of renewable energy, effectively storing value in products rather than relying solely on batteries.
2. He also predicts that sooner or later, buildings will incorporate at least some battery storage, making it as common as other basic appliances.
3. Through local energy management and storage, buildings will gain control over their energy use, independent of when power is supplied by the grid.
4. An interesting goal for energy startups could be to provide their integrated heating and energy storage systems "as a service," eliminating the initial investment barrier for users and closing the loop on the value proposition.

But as he emphasizes, ideas alone are not enough to make a business. Operational details make the difference.

## Sustainable digital entrepreneurship

**Digital entrepreneurship** broadly refers to the process of creating new or innovative internet-based companies, products, or services. This definition encompasses both startups introducing new digital offerings to the market and the digital transformation of existing business activities within established companies or the public sector. Digital entrepreneurship thus connects entrepreneurship with technological development, creativity, innovation, and the acquisition of digital skills. The core of digital entrepreneurship lies in leveraging digital tools and platforms to create value and opportunities, and this shift enables unprecedented scalability and reach - which has significant implications for the potential impact of sustainable solutions.

In the intersection between digital entrepreneurship and sustainable development, we find the concept of *digital sustainable entrepreneurship*: a business model perspective that integrates digital technologies with the aim of creating social and environmental value.

### Business model innovation

A practical starting point is the Business Model Canvas (BMC): the familiar one-page map of customer segments, value propositions, channels, revenue streams, key resources, activities, partners and costs. For a sustainable digital venture, two extensions matter. First, the *value proposition* must be honest about who pays and who benefits - including nature and future generations as silent stakeholders, in the spirit of chapter 4's non-human personas. Second, the cost and revenue logic must survive the "green premium" test: will customers pay for the sustainability, or must the model win on ordinary merits (savings, convenience, quality) with sustainability as the bonus? As the Lancey case and the barriers section below show, models that *require* customers to pay extra for virtue are fragile; models where sustainability and self-interest align are robust.

### Strategies for acquiring funding

Europe has seen a wave of tech startups combining digital innovation with green causes - from energy management to circular economy. In fact, Europe is home to roughly twice as many *climate tech* startups as the US (30,000 vs. 14,300), despite struggling with limited venture funding ([World Fund](https://www.worldfund.vc/knowledge/white-paper-climate-tech-for-european-resilience)). Funding routes range from classic venture capital, through dedicated ESG and impact funds, to bootstrapping - growing on revenue without external investors. Each carries a governance consequence: venture capital typically demands growth rates that can conflict with sufficiency thinking, impact funds demand measurable outcomes, and bootstrapping preserves autonomy at the price of speed. Choosing a funding model is choosing a set of masters; a sustainable venture should choose masters whose incentives it can live with.

### Value chains and ecosystems

No digital product exists alone. Chapter 1 introduced digital ecosystems, and chapter 3 mapped the physical supply chains behind hardware; the entrepreneurial task is to know your own position in these chains. A typical industrial-digital value chain runs from a *device layer* (sensors on equipment), through a *connectivity layer* (networks transmitting data) and a *platform layer* (cloud services storing and organizing it), to an *analytics layer* (models detecting patterns) and an *application layer* (dashboards and feedback to operators). Each layer has its own sustainability profile - and its own dependency risks, which is where the digital sovereignty perspective from chapter 8 re-enters: open standards, open source and lifecycle assessment tools such as openLCA (chapter 5) keep options open. Stakeholder mapping completes the picture: who, human and non-human, is affected at each layer?

## Sustainability by IT: cases from the European twin transition

Digital technologies such as AI, IoT, and data analytics can help other industries reduce their CO2 emissions; analyses suggest that digitalization can enable a reduction of up to 20% of global emissions by 2030 ([DigitalEurope](https://www.digitaleurope.org/resources/digital-action-climate-action-8-ideas-to-accelerate-the-twin-transition/)). This makes tech entrepreneurs key players in the green transition. The examples below - all European - show the breadth of the field. As always, remember the critical questions from chapter 2: reported savings are claims to be examined, not verdicts.

**Energy: Next Kraftwerke (Germany)** operates one of Europe's largest *virtual power plants*. Through a digital platform, the company has aggregated thousands of decentralized renewable units - wind turbines, solar parks, biogas plants, batteries - with a combined capacity of several coal plants, trading and balancing them as one power plant via cloud software ([Clean Energy Wire](https://www.cleanenergywire.org/news/start-next-kraftwerkes-renewable-virtual-power-plant-stabilises-grid)). It is a textbook example of software substituting for fossil infrastructure.

**Cities and industry:** In Vienna, a smart city project in a residential block has reduced CO2 emissions by 71% using data and digital management systems; in the Port of Rotterdam, AI-optimized ship routing and port calls are expected to halve the port's CO2 emissions by 2030; and an AI tool installed in a Belgian wind farm has increased the renewable energy delivered to the grid by roughly 5-6% ([DigitalEurope](https://www.digitaleurope.org/resources/digital-action-climate-action-8-ideas-to-accelerate-the-twin-transition/)).

**Food waste: Too Good To Go (Denmark)** connects consumers with restaurants, bakeries and supermarkets that have surplus food. Within three and a half years of its 2016 launch it had saved 29 million meals and avoided more than 72,000 tons of CO2 - equivalent to taking 15,000 cars off the road for a year - and the model has since scaled internationally ([Zero Waste Europe](https://zerowasteeurope.eu/wp-content/uploads/2020/01/zero_waste_europe_CS7_CP_TooGoodToGo_en.pdf)).

**Finance: Doconomy (Sweden)** converts payment transactions into CO2 estimates, giving consumers and banks insight into the climate impact of consumption; the company has attracted substantial investment to scale its impact-fintech tools ([ESG Today](https://www.esgtoday.com/climate-impact-fintech-doconomy-raises-37-million-to-help-consumers-make-sustainable-choices/)). Its DO card - a credit card with a CO2 ceiling that stops purchases when the user's climate budget is spent - remains a thought-provoking piece of loyal-pattern design ([Thales Group](https://www.thalesgroup.com/en/markets/digital-identity-and-security/banking-payment/inspired/doconomy)). Among established players, the Dutch **Triodos Bank** has financed only projects with positive environmental or social impact since its founding and publishes every loan for full transparency.

**Health: telemedicine.** The healthcare sector accounts for a significant share of society's resource consumption, but digital health can help: a systematic review of 48 studies found that roughly 68 million telemedicine consultations saved approximately 691,000 tons of CO2 and 3.3 billion kilometers of travel ([European Urology Focus 2023](https://www.eu-focus.europeanurology.com/article/S2405-4569(23)00272-9/abstract)). Platforms such as Sweden's Kry/Livi have delivered millions of online consultations, and MedTech Europe argues that climate and health policy should be designed together, with digitally supported, resource-efficient care pathways ([MedTech Europe 2025](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf)).

**And many more:** search engines that fund reforestation (Ecosia, Germany), data centers placed inside wind turbines (windCORES, Germany), marketplaces for refurbished electronics (Back Market, France), CO2-accounting SaaS platforms (Plan A, Envirly and others), plastic-to-raw-material recycling (DePoly, Switzerland) and AI-verified ocean plastic recovery (CleanHub, Germany). The pattern across all of them: digital tools applied to measurement, matching and optimization problems that markets alone were not solving.

## Leadership in sustainable digital projects

Governance provides structure; leadership provides direction. A few principles recur among the leaders of the companies in this chapter and this book:

- **Focus on core strengths.** Fairphone did not try to fix everything at once; it chose a few material flows and worked them relentlessly. Leaders of sustainable digital projects choose battles where their organization can genuinely move the needle.
- **Lead regeneratively.** Regenerative leadership applies chapter 4's design principle to organizations: leave the teams, communities and ecosystems you touch in better condition than you found them - which includes the work-life sustainability from chapter 1.
- **Navigate standards deliberately.** The certifications and frameworks from earlier chapters - B Corp, EcoVadis, SustainableIT, SCI, CSRD disclosure topics - are leadership tools: they translate ambition into commitments that outlive individual enthusiasm.
- **Mind the human factor.** Transitions fail on culture, not spreadsheets. The IT-Branchen recommendations in chapter 6 put it plainly: involve key employees from all areas, and invest in green skills.

Leadership sets direction; governance builds structure; entrepreneurship delivers impact. All three are needed.

## Barriers and pitfalls

Although the potential is great, there are well-known barriers that can slow digital entrepreneurship in the sustainable transition.

**Market pressure and economics.** Green business models must be able to compete economically. An illustrative example is the Berlin startup **Infarm**, which specialized in digitally controlled vertical farming. Infarm grew rapidly and raised almost $500 million in capital, but had to declare insolvency in 2023, as the business model could not sustain the high energy costs and the "green premium" price that indoor plant production entailed - traditional greenhouses had sunlight for free ([Sifted](https://sifted.eu/articles/infarm-raised-500m-and-disappeared)). Even groundbreaking digital innovation cannot outrun an economic model that does not add up.

**Access to capital.** Europe's venture capital level has traditionally been lower than the US's - roughly 0.2% of GDP against 0.7% over the last decade ([The Next Web](https://thenextweb.com/news/europe-has-twice-as-many-climate-tech-startups-as-us)). Europe produces many green tech companies but struggles to fund their scaling, pushing some to seek capital - and eventually jobs and IP - outside the EU. Programs like InvestEU attempt to close the gap, but the financing shortfall remains a real constraint on the 2030 goals.

**Regulation and incentives.** Regulation cuts both ways. Complex approval procedures and diverging national rules burden startups, and in some sectors existing rules actively work against sustainable digital solutions - MedTech Europe points, for example, to EU requirements for paper instructions where electronic ones would save material, and to health budgets that do not reward preventive digital care because the gains land in other silos ([MedTech Europe 2025](https://www.medtecheurope.org/wp-content/uploads/2025/01/241220_medtech_europe_clean-industrial-deal_final.pdf)). Weak market signals - a low carbon price, timid green public procurement - let cheaper, less sustainable alternatives win.

**Skills and people.** Europe is estimated to need at least 20 million ICT specialists by 2030 to drive the digital transition ([BFNA](https://www.bfna.org/politics-society/the-future-of-work-in-the-twin-transition-to-green-and-digital-3xn0a3queo/)). If education systems and companies fail to develop and retain talent in software, data and green technology, the twin transition stalls on human resources.

**The deeper tension.** Finally, recall chapter 8: there is an inherent tension between the "logic of limits" in the green transition and the "logic of unlimited possibilities" in the digital one. Digital solutions can reinforce unsustainable consumption, their own footprint can outweigh their gains, and the materials behind green technology carry extractive risks of their own. A truly sustainable twin transition demands resource efficiency, circularity and ethical vigilance - not just enthusiasm.

## Future prospects for the digital and green transition

The EU's vision for 2030 contains ambitious goals for both transformations. Under *Europe's Digital Decade*, targets include that 75% of EU companies should use cloud, AI or big data, and that 10,000 climate-neutral, highly secure edge data centers should be established across Europe ([European Commission](https://commission.europa.eu/strategy-and-policy/priorities-2019-2024/europe-fit-digital-age/europes-digital-decade-digital-targets-2030_en)). These digital goals are designed to go hand in hand with the Green Deal objectives of 55% CO2 reduction by 2030 and climate neutrality by 2050, and the EU's recovery fund required member states to allocate at least 37% of budgets to green and 20% to digital initiatives ([European Commission](https://commission.europa.eu/business-economy-euro/economic-recovery/recovery-and-resilience-facility_en)) - a concrete expression of the twin transition thought of as one. Estimates put the required investment at up to 745 billion euros towards 2030 ([DigitalEurope](https://www.digitaleurope.org/news/joint-statement-a-call-to-action-for-a-digital-single-market-by-2030/)).

For entrepreneurs and practitioners, the signal is clear: capital, regulation and demand are converging on the intersection of digital and green. New ventures in green AI, climate finance, energy storage, smart cities and circular economy will find both opportunity and scrutiny there - scrutiny being the operative word, as the EmpCo rules from chapter 1 raise the price of unsubstantiated claims.

## Bringing it together: Key insights from this chapter

Sustainability in digital projects does not happen by itself; it requires intentional governance that aligns people, policies and technologies. The journey runs from compliance to strategy: from documenting what regulators demand to deciding what long-term value the organization exists to create. Stories shape culture, but strategy - anchored in measured indicators and honest value propositions - sustains outcomes.

The distinction between sustainability *by* IT and *of* IT keeps organizations honest: celebrate the emissions your platform saves, but account for the footprint it costs. The European cases show that the twin transition is a genuine business landscape, and the Lancey and Infarm stories show it is an unforgiving one: resilience and operational discipline decide who survives to deliver impact.

Leadership sets direction; governance builds structure; entrepreneurship delivers impact. Whichever of the three is yours to give, the preceding chapters have supplied the tools - and the next, final chapter looks at where the road leads.
