# 3. The impact of digitalization on environment and society

This chapter discusses the tension between the negative impacts of digitalization and its benefits to society. It provides insight into how IT and digitalization affect the environment and today's society.

The chapter first discusses sustainability in relation to hardware, then networks, and finally software's relationship to sustainability. As an introduction to the chapter, we invite you to consider a concrete example of how modern technology can cause pollution with unprecedented global consequences. Let's look at the story of how space technology has transformed Earth's orbit from a pristine area to an explosive dumping ground in just a few decades.
## How earth's orbit became polluted so fast

We are increasingly learning that when new technologies surge onto the global stage, the consequences of neglecting long-term thinking can manifest quickly. Consider the pollution of space around Earth – a potent illustration of how rapid technological growth, pursued without adequate environmental foresight, can breed global problems.

For eons, the space above Earth was pristine. Then came the Space Age in the 1950s. Tragically, within just a few decades, human activity turned Earth's orbit into a dangerous dumping ground. From the late 1950s through the 1980s, satellites were launched with minimal consideration for end-of-life disposal or the orbital environment. Expired satellites and mission-related fragments were left adrift, creating a growing cloud of hazardous "space junk."

Currently, more than 8,000 satellites circle our planet, enabling modern communication, observation, and navigation. But this orbital infrastructure, along with future space endeavors, is increasingly endangered by debris from past missions. Hurtling around Earth at immense speeds, even small pieces of debris can cause devastating collisions.

The most alarming possibility is a runaway chain reaction: collisions creating more debris, leading to more collisions, and so on. Such a cascade could potentially cripple essential services like GPS and global communications, and close off access to space for future generations ([Kelvey 2024](https://aerospaceamerica.aiaa.org/features/understanding-the-misunderstood-kessler-syndrome/)). Yet, hope remains that a shift towards more sustainable, long-view practices can help us manage this orbital challenge.
### Towards sustainable operations in space

Recognizing the threat posed by space debris, the space industry has increasingly focused on sustainable, long-term solutions in recent years. The goal is to mitigate orbital hazards and ensure the continued safe use of space for satellite deployment (Frank 2023).

Several different initiatives are underway to address this challenge and secure the future availability of satellite technologies:

* **_Lifecycle management_:** Satellites are now being designed with their **entire lifecycle** in mind. The satellites are now designed to prevent them from ending up as space debris at the end of their mission.

* **_Downsizing_:** We use smaller and cheaper micro- and nanosatellites, which are small and compact, use fewer resources overall, and are generally easier to manage.

* **_Iterative Development_:** Historically, satellites often had long development cycles (10-15 years) followed by equally long service lives (10-15 years), resulting in operational technology potentially being decades old. Given the rapid pace of technological advancement, the industry is moving towards shorter lifecycles. This approach allows deployed satellites to incorporate more current and efficient technologies, improving performance and potentially reducing mission duration.

But what is the connection between satellites and sustainable IT? Satellites are crucial for numerous functions underpinning our digital world. These include enabling global **data transfer**, **global positioning (GPS)**, facilitating **Earth observation** (including imaging), and supporting vital scientific **research**, among other key applications. Key global communication services depend on this orbital infrastructure. Therefore, the destruction of satellites by space debris doesn't just affect space exploration; it could severely disrupt vital digital communication systems worldwide, highlighting a critical sustainability vulnerability.
## What can we learn from the recent developments in satellite technology?

The story of satellite pollution powerfully illustrates how rapidly new technologies can generate global environmental problems. Yet, it also offers a crucial lesson: sustainable pathways become possible when we commit to addressing the long-term consequences of innovation. Core sustainability principles highlighted by the space debris challenge – including *Lifecycle Management*, *Downsizing*, and *Iterative Development* – offer valuable frameworks applicable across the entire information technology landscape.

Space industry can inspire us in informatics to integrate these (and similar other) principles into the design and management of product lifecycles. All development must be grounded in sustainability, emphasizing circular economy approaches like cradle-to-cradle recycling.  Ultimately, we must actively mitigate the unintended negative consequences of IT by designing solutions with lifecycles, that deliver lasting benefits for users, the environment, and society.

Fundamentally, all IT systems consist of three core components: **hardware**, **software**, and **network infrastructure.** Crucially, there is also the **human factor, the users,** who interact with these systems. ## Sustainability aspects of hardware

There is no digitalization or information technology without hardware and electronics. Computers, smartphones and networking equipment are built from many different parts, such as printed circuit boards, microchips, plastic and electronic components.

The production of hardware requires high-tech production lines and many different materials, such as plastics, metals and rare earths, and it is often global trade and cooperation that makes it possible to produce state-of-the-art systems. Currently, electronics are mostly produced in the Far East, but in time many countries will want to have their own hardware production (e.g. chip production) because we have experienced how vulnerable it is when global supply lines stop and we cannot access modern technology.

The illustration below shows how the supply network for a smartphone works. Minerals and raw materials from all over the world end up in China, where they are assembled into a product that is eventually sold in Europe.

![Figure 3.2](resources/I_3.2.png)

Figure 3.2 From responsible raw material extraction to production and distribution: Fairphone's global supply chains visualized through Sourcemap. By focusing on the entire chain, Fairphone promotes fairer and more environmentally conscious production.[[1]](#_ftn1)

It is important that IT projects are based on sustainably developed and produced hardware, as the manufacturing of phones with their many components often has a negative climate footprint. An example of a more sustainable phone is Fairphone 4, whose components include gold from Peru, plastic from the EU and USA and tungsten from Rwanda (as illustrated in Figure 3.2). But the manufacturer makes a point of mapping and understanding its supply chains to best produce cell phones from sustainable materials.

In this specific example, the company is taking initiatives to improve mining in Africa and working conditions in Asia. The company is trying to take responsibility for making their smartphones more sustainable than the standard, even though we are very far from being able to make truly sustainable smartphones.

Unfortunately, there are still only a few hardware manufacturers that take as sustainable an approach to hardware production as Fairphone , but more are on the way. Nevertheless, a prerequisite for a more sustainable hardware production is to be able to procure your materials and components in a way that takes into account their impact on nature, the environment and the surrounding community.

### A sustainable hardware lifecycle

The average lifespan of business computer equipment is around three to five years, after which companies often expect to buy new. In reality, hardware can last a little longer (possibly with repairs), but technological developments mean that in many cases it pays to replace old hardware with new to significantly improve performance, reduce power consumption and reduce the risk of failure.

In order to move hardware production in a more sustainable direction, we need to consider environmental, societal, human and economic aspects in our IT projects. Best practices for sustainable hardware procurement and recycling are summarized in figure 3.3.

### Electronic waste and recycling

Raw materials and components used in hardware should be sourced and produced as sustainably as possible. Some IT equipment can be reused directly after refurbishment, where the hardware is cleaned, refreshed and put back into use.

The rest of the material should be sorted and recycled responsibly, so that (close to) all raw materials are reused either for hardware production or for something else. Hardware manufacturers can play a role here by motivating their customers to return used hardware and ensure proper disassembly and recycling.

Most of a computer's materials can be recycled, either as direct reuse of its components (refurbished) or as recycling of raw materials. Unfortunately, only a very small proportion of our hardware is still being recycled as intended (17.4% globally), so we can really make a difference by promoting proper recycling of electronic parts and reducing electronic scrap (United Nations University 2024).

#### Longevity: extended lifespan

The amount of electronic scrap can be reduced by extending the lifetime of the hardware through e.g. repair. At a European level, various legislative proposals are being worked on to make electronics more sustainable, including obliging manufacturers to provide better opportunities to repair products (Jakobsen 2023).

In this context, the fact that microchips are getting faster every year is a problem. While it's generally a good idea to keep a computer alive for longer by repairing it, this is not necessarily the case for the server. It's more sustainable to scrap old power-hungry servers for a newer model that is significantly more efficient.

In terms of energy consumption, hardware manufacturing should strive to reduce energy consumption and use renewable energy in all stages of both production and recycling to lower the use of fossil fuels (and thus reduce greenhouse gas emissions). It should be remembered that water and air used in production must also be cleaned before it is released from the factory.

Last but not least, there are huge green gains to be made if manufacturers use the latest knowledge and sustainable designs in their products. A concrete example of this is processor technology (the processor is the "brain" of the computer).

There are different types of processors that can perform the same calculations but use more or less power. The so-called _ARM processors_ use less energy for their calculations than equivalent traditional processors, and this can help software running on ARM processors to provide better energy efficiency .

### Consideration for people and communities

Responsible business practices are also a focal point when it comes to hardware production. In recent years, we have seen shocking examples of how electronics manufacturers - typically subcontractors in countries with less regulated labor markets - have exploited their workers to the extent that it has led to tragic events such as suicides in factories ("Foxconn Suicides" 2024). In some cases, workers have been subjected to extremely long working days, low wages, lack of rights and intense pressure to maintain a high production rate. These conditions are far from what we are used to in Danish-owned workplaces, and they show how brutal global competition can be if proper conditions are not demanded.

At all stages of production, hardware manufacturers should therefore strive to show consideration for the workforce involved in the production of hardware products. This can include ethical sourcing of components, supply chain transparency and continuous monitoring of the working environment. At the same time, companies should also consider how their presence and operations affect the local communities around their factories - not only to avoid scandal, but to contribute to a more responsible and sustainable global division of labor.

### Hardware manufacturers' green promises

Our commitment to become carbon neutral by 2030 drives everything we do. Every Apple product will be made with clean energy and even more recycled and renewable materials. (Apple 2024)

Samsung Electronics is making efforts to move away from a linear economy of disposable resource consumption and transition to a circular economy that utilizes resources efficiently by reusing and recycling more_._ (Samsung 2024)

Since the launch of our first hardware products, we've worked to improve the sustainability of how we make our products and the services we create around them [...] from sourcing materials to packaging products to designing our retail stores_._ (Google 2023)

The above quotes are examples of hardware manufacturers increasing their focus on sustainability. It is positive that hardware manufacturers are making such environmental promises, but it can be difficult to determine whether this is a genuine green agenda or some degree of greenwashing .

The fact that companies choose to focus on sustainability is important because with our current technological capabilities it is close to impossible to develop truly sustainable hardware products. It will take several decades to develop a fully circular information technology and this can only be achieved if manufacturers continuously show progress and improvements year after year.

As long as we have to extract new minerals for every product, we're making an ever-greater dent in the earth's limited resources. Without a circular approach, we could run out of minerals such as lithium or rare earth elements, just as we run out of fossil fuels. Conversely, if we succeed in recycling minerals and raw materials so that a new hardware product consists of 99.99% recycled materials, we can talk about sustainable hardware production, because in principle we can continue to produce new hardware for as long as we want.

While it is unfortunately impossible with our current technology to develop completely sustainable hardware, you can still advance your organization's overall sustainability by considering the above hardware aspects in your IT projects. You can further the sustainability agenda in your practical work by asking questions about the aforementioned aspects of the hardware part of the projects you are involved in. ## Sustainability aspects of networking and cloud

Modern digital technology consists not only of computers, smartphones and printers, but also of the (data) network itself, which forms a large part of the technology. Because what can you do with a computer, smartphone or server without a network connection? You can't exchange information with others or retrieve data from other devices. That's why it's essential to consider the network when talking about sustainability and IT.

Figure 3.4 illustrates how regular use of a computer (e.g. browsing or chatting) sends data out into the world and gets data back. Every click on a link in a browser will send data out into the world in this way and retrieve a response back. To understand the environmental impact of your information technology, you also need to consider the network's part in the carbon footprint.


![Figure 3.4](resources/I_3.4.png)

Figure 3.4 Data flows through many different nodes on the network before it reaches the end user.

As shown in Figure 3.4, the data network consists of interconnected network devices such as computers, routers and switches that can carry data between the connected devices using copper cables, fiber optics or radio signals such as Wi-Fi and Bluetooth. The world's largest e[[A3]](#_msocom_3)  IT network is the Internet, but there are also other networks such as mobile networks or smaller, private networks.

The internet allows us to move and process data over the network instead of moving data via floppy disks or other physical media, which means that data transmission over the network is happening all the time and therefore has a significant environmental impact. There are large and small data centers all over the world that use large amounts of electronics and electrical energy - both for data transfer, but also for cooling all the heat produced by the hardware in the data centers.

### Network energy consumption and CO2 emissions

Large data centers and Internet Service Providers (ISPs) have always had a financial incentive to lower energy consumption in their networks and data centers as electricity bills increase with traffic and data volumes. Every penny saved on energy is a win for them.

The current network is based on electronic data transmission through metal cables, such as copper cables, which will eventually be replaced by fiber optics. Modern fiber optics use less energy than older network technologies and are becoming increasingly widespread (Europacapable 2022).

Data travels through different nodes and online services are located in different places and use different hardware equipment, making the environmental impact of the network difficult to quantify. Nevertheless, efforts are underway to estimate the energy consumption and CO2 emissions of the software services.

A concrete example is a proposal from French Bertrand Martin to introduce an HTTP Response Header Field named Carbon-Emissions-Scope-2. This would make it possible to provide an estimate of how much CO2 emissions a given internet transfer has caused (Martin 2023). Although the proposal has not gained widespread acceptance, it is a good example of measures that can make emissions from data transfer more visible.

### The cloud can lead the way for good climate solutions

Another concept that is relevant to networking _is cloud computing_.

Let's illustrate the concept with an example. When you take a picture with a cell phone, the image is stored on the phone itself, i.e. in the phone's memory. You can also choose to store the image on a server (a specific computer in a data center). In both cases, you will know where the image is physically located - either on the phone or on the server at a given location.

But on newer phones, the image is automatically uploaded to the cloud. The cloud is a huge network of servers covering the globe. Here we can't point to a single computer or server that has the image file, but instead we leave the responsibility of storing the image in a suitable location to the cloud provider. The cloud provider's systems will automatically determine where the image is stored in the world (Frankfurt? Singapore?) and what media the image should be stored on (expensive but fast media, or cheap and slow media?).

The more knowledge suppliers have about specific technologies, the more they can automate the optimization of sustainability criteria for network and cloud usage. According to a Greenpeace report, there is hope for the energy consumption of IT networks and the internet as major market players are actively working to make large data centers more sustainable. For example, by locating new data centers in areas with renewable energy sources and entering into partnerships with sustainability goals (Greenpeace International 2017).

### Societal aspects of networking

You can connect a few computers in a small network called a _LAN_ (Local Area Network). When many computers and multiple networks are connected together, it's called a WAN (Wide Area Network). The Internet is the largest network ever and it connects billions of devices in one giant system . You might get the impression that the internet never goes out and that everyone can always access all sources of information on demand. However, it is important to note that this is not always the case.

In some parts of the world, there are restrictions on the internet. China's network infrastructure is separated from the rest of the world by the Great Firewall, which blocks network traffic from selected sources. China uses the firewall to censor the internet by preventing access to unwanted foreign services. The purpose of the firewall is to control the flow of information within the country to better control the population. Network technology also makes it possible to block unwanted websites in Denmark and the EU (Strarup 2022).

Many of the basic facilities of the internet were originally designed in an American context. But as the internet goes global, efforts are being made to regulate and govern it through global and democratic instruments and organizations where all countries can be represented. International non-profit organizations such as ICANN, IANA and W3C may be based in the US, but they collaborate with all countries around the world on the allocation of domain names (TLDs), IP addresses and MAC addresses on a global level.

It is in the interest of the global community that the Internet remains neutral, open and accessible to all, and that no companies or countries dominate development. From a democratic and Western perspective, it is also essential that citizens can access information online and that the information available has a high truth value.

#### When the network connects us - or separates us

But networks are also vulnerable. Denial of service attacks are very common. This can put certain parts of the network out of action for a short period of time. Fortunately, the internet is designed to be robust and resilient to outages. This feature makes it possible to be connected all the time, both at home and at work. The network makes remote working possible, changing people's lives for better and for worse. Some thrive in the home office, while others need a physical presence for effective collaboration.

Areas without internet connectivity can suffer from being cut off from the global information highway, while others seek the same off-grid areas where they can be cut off from the outside world. So the very development and deployment of networking technologies can have knock-on effects that affect both society and individuals.

Finally, it should be mentioned that computer networks are also built on hardware and therefore we should include the sustainability issues discussed in the previous section on hardware.

## Sustainability aspects of software

Software is a collective term for the programs and applications that run on computer hardware. This includes everything from word processors, websites and webshops to smartphone apps, databases, chatbots, servers, electric car management and factory control software.

Software controls the interaction between all hardware and network components and is therefore crucial in all digitalization projects. Good and efficient software can bring significant benefits, while inefficient and poor software can pose a direct threat to digitalization.

Effective software optimizes work processes, improves user experience and increases productivity, while poor software can lead to system errors, security issues and inefficiencies, which can hamper growth and development. That's why it's essential to focus on the quality of software in any digitalization effort.

### The software has an impact on power consumption and

Software running on hardware has a big impact on how much electricity IT systems use. Well-designed software is efficient and requires only the necessary resources, while poor software can use excessive computing resources, increasing the power consumption of the device.

Researchers around the world are working to find new ways to measure and optimize software power consumption, but there is no standardized label or method to measure software power consumption.[[2]](#_ftn2) As a rule of thumb, the less computing power the software uses to perform a given function and the smaller the amount of data it needs to process, the lower the power consumption. However, it's important to remember that computers always have a significant base power consumption when they are turned on, regardless of the software running on them.

This makes it difficult to measure how much energy is used for a given software solution. In chapter 2, we mentioned that simple digital actions, such as watching a video online, have a hidden and indirect energy consumption because the video is streamed from a server over the network every time someone wants to watch it, and both the server and the network have an energy consumption associated with the streaming.

Another example of the hidden power consumption of software is artificial intelligence (AI). Training the large AI models that have to chew through large amounts of data and use the computing power of thousands of GPUs simultaneously  extremely energy-intensive (Olifent 2024). Raghavendra Selvan from DTU has estimated that 0.1938 kWh is used every time a prompt is sent to ChatGPT (Kristensen 2024). This is roughly equivalent to charging a smartphone 40 times.

From a larger perspective, we can assume that some of the newly developed and advanced algorithms will be more energy-intensive in the future than we can imagine today. We also see this development blockchains and AI - and in both cases, the technologies were born with a huge energy hunger, with an energy consumption comparable to that of a medium-sized European country. While these technologies have become more energy efficient as they have evolved, the rebound effect can cause the total energy consumption of these technologies to increase despite the efficiency improvements.

Research also shows that it can make a big difference which programming languages you use to develop your software. The rule of thumb is that minimalist and specialized frameworks use fewer resources than large generic frameworks.[[3]](#_ftn3)

### Technical debt and code optimization

An often overlooked sustainability issue software development is technical debt . Technical debt can be briefly described as the quick and unsustainable shortcuts in the development process that result in large additional future costs. It's a bit like a building: it's important to build a strong foundation because the rest must rest on and be supported by the foundation, which cannot be changed afterwards.

An example in software development: an algorithm needs to handle large amounts of data. If a less careful developer takes the first and best program snippet from Stack Overflow[[4]](#_ftn4) to solve the problem, it will most likely not be the most efficient solution. The developer incurs a debt to the future, because the application will use more resources than necessary and will cost more in terms of environment, economy and security.

The same algorithm can, with due diligence, good knowledge of algorithm efficiency, refactoring and testing, be programmed more efficiently, using only a fraction of the resources. The developer can be assisted by various tools that can be used to further optimize the code to meet the best practices of the programming language in question.

A concrete example of technical debt is a large company that sends large amounts of real-time data from cars on the road to central servers. Sending data is relatively costly because they have to pay per gigabyte transferred. While the developers have reduced the size of the data with a standard compression to save on the data transfer bill, by using a customized compression that is significantly more efficient for car data than the standard solution, the company could save several million dollars annually. In this way, the company would have eliminated their technical debt in terms of compression.

#### Choose software packages wisely

Technical debt can also manifest itself when choosing frameworks or software packages as the starting point for a project. It is a common practice to use open source systems or libraries to develop a system , which in reality ends up forming the bulk of the solution. The website software WordPress (a Content Management System) is a good example of a software platform that you can build on.

WordPress is known for its popular "five-minute install". You start from scratch and within five minutes you can set up a fully functional website with advanced features. WordPress is both open source and free, and it is truly a great system for relatively simple websites and web solutions. There are also many companies that build their operations on WordPress, as it can be expanded with plugins and serve as a blog, CMS or webshop.

The problems arise when projects "outgrow WordPress" because new demands on the system require more customized algorithms than WordPress and its ecosystem can offer. WordPress is a generalized system that performs many tasks well, but it can fall short in terms of performance and functionality when it needs to be optimized for specific tasks that it was not designed for. It's not just WordPress that has this disadvantage; the same goes for most similar systems because generic systems have a hard time being optimized when they have to perform very specific tasks.

This means that technical debt can arise when using systems, frameworks or libraries that are not optimized for a given task. Therefore, it's wise to choose software and components with a focus on performance optimization and sustainability.

If you are already working with solutions that have a significant technical debt, you can pay off the debt, so to speak, by optimizing existing code and architecture. A practical example of this is the use of Varnish Cache, which helps reduce developers' technical debt. Varnish Cache is an application that can increase the speed of web solutions by up to 300-1,000 times, depending on the architecture.

By using Varnish, companies can significantly reduce their server count and resource consumption. This results in huge savings, both financially and environmentally. However, it's also important to point out that there are limits to how much you can optimize bad solutions. Sometimes it's simply not worth "making up a corpse" because fundamental flaws in the system will still hinder optimal performance and sustainability. It may be more efficient to switch to a new, well-structured solution that is more durable.

### Individual and societal aspects of software

Software affects much more than just power consumption and CO2 emissions. On the one hand, software needs to be designed smartly so that hardware and networks use as few resources as possible. But on the other hand, software opens up huge opportunities to create a more sustainable world.

With the right software, we can optimize existing products and processes in a more sustainable direction. For example, software control of heating and ventilation in buildings can significantly reduce energy consumption. Engineers use advanced software solutions to calculate more efficient solutions to various problems, while AI solutions can help design entirely new sustainable products and processes. Additionally, software can influence user behavior by training and educating people in more sustainable practices.

Software impacts the people who use it. Often these influences are beneficial and help us perform our activities more efficiently. For example, social media makes it easy to send messages to selected people. However, there are also side effects; for example, excessive use of digital media can lead to mental health issues. In the right hands, software can create positive change, but it's also easy to abuse it for reckless or harmful purposes. Software can be designed to utilize psychological tricks to get users to do what the designer wants - for better or worse.

### Addiction in a sustainable perspective

As we as a society use more and more digital devices, the same digitalization creates an increased dependency in the individual. This addiction affects daily habits, mental life, work life and social relationships. To understand the scale of the problem, it's worth looking at digital dependency in an ordinary everyday life. It's not just extreme cases or harmful niche phenomena that affect us negatively; our daily interaction with smartphones, social platforms and continuous information delivery also creates unwanted experiences. The constant stream of digital impressions can make us distracted and reduce our ability to immerse ourselves. Both young and old find it harder to stay focused, exercise critical thinking and engage with deeper political and cultural issues. Digital addiction is therefore not just a problem for the few. On the contrary, it is gradually reshaping our culture, our way of being and our way of life.

_Attention theft_a term Camilla Mehlsen uses in her book of the same title to describe how digital companies purposefully exploit our urges and immediate reaction patterns to steal and hold our attention. Companies use dark patterns, such as user retention algorithms, design tricks and emotionally appealing content to constantly draw users towards small, immediate rewards, at the expense of immersion, critical thinking and community engagement. These attention-sucking algorithms and dark patterns can keep users stuck in habits and situations that do not benefit them or are downright harmful. Therefore, in the name of sustainability, we should consider the individual well-being of users in all digital development projects in the same way we consider environmental effects.

### Product lifespan , consumerism and low tech

Digital development also has the effect of increasing consumerism. As new applications often do not work optimally on older hardware, we may be tempted to buy new digital devices in order to use the latest features and applications, even if they do not offer any improvements compared to previous versions or in terms of need and usability.

Manufacturers have a vested interest in selling new products continuously and often find that their products are designed not to last long. _Planned_ obsolescence means that the product design promotes degradation of the product rather than an opportunity to repair and upgrade it.

By prioritizing design for repairability and longevity (longevity ), we can counteract this practice and promote a more sustainable technological development. The longer the software supports a given hardware configuration, the better. In the open source _environment, STS_ (Short-term support ) and _LTS_ (Long-term support ) are used to indicate how long a specific software version will be updated.

STS versions contain new, more experimental features that need to be tested on a large scale before developers can determine if they are also durable in the long run. In contrast, there are LTS versions of software that promise long-term durability. Here, only proven and established software features are included in the released version. This means that the LTS version is slightly less capable (has fewer features) than the STS version, but will last longer - in some cases ten years or more. You could also argue that LTS represents a lower technological level than STS (= is low tech), but for that very reason the LTS version is more suitable for long-term use.

It is an ongoing debate whether to move towards more low-tech lifestyles and IT solutions with longer lifespans and therefore lower consumption, or to focus more on circulation and repair of existing devices. This book suggests following the latter path, promoting designs that enable easy repair and upgrading of existing hardware and software so that users do not need to buy new devices as often.

### Activism, politics and war in cyberspace

As we strive for sustainability in the technology industry, we also face complex tasks in cyberspace, where activism, politics and cyberwar dominate the agenda. The internet is a great communication platform where anyone can talk to anyone, even secretly (encrypted) if you choose the right services. Secret communication channels open up opportunities to use digital technologies in their activism, although the vast majority of activism and political engagement takes place on open lines and channels.

Free information systems are very important in democratic societies where citizens should be able to freely communicate with each other and exchange information. A clear example of how people could organize themselves online was seen during the protests of the Arab Spring 2010-11. The protests largely resonated on social media and online channels (The National 2012), and social platforms helped amplify messages and mobilize different actors in the political uprising. Governments tried to prevent the use of social channels - with varying degrees of success.

Totalitarian leaders have since realized that not controlling digital media, and the internet in particular, could damage their power. Some countries are content to disconnect themselves from the global internet and control the web their borders. China has the Great Firewall, which filters all international traffic so that Chinese people cannot look abroad. Russia has recently conducted a series of tests on how to disconnect the Russian part of the internet from the rest of the world. This will make Russia self-sustaining, like a closed Russian network (Andersen 2021).

Dictatorships themselves use technologies such as surveillance, facial recognition and autonomous systems worldwide to oppress populations and maintain control. Social media is misused to influence the public through propaganda and disinformation .[[A6]](#_msocom_6) [[A7]](#_msocom_7) 

Most major powers now have digital protection systems and attack capabilities and can carry out destructive cyberattacks worldwide. The internet is therefore no longer just a source of global information, but also a battleground in a global hybrid war. As a consequence, sustainable information systems must be characterized by digital resilience, which can form effective defenses against misuse of the systems. Therefore, information security is also a cornerstone of digital sustainability work.

## How can we make information technology more sustainable?

In the book _Sustainable Business Studies_ 2024, we have outlined two basic strategies for sustainability improvements; the _reactive_ and the _proactive_ strategies (Juel-Jacobsen 2024)_._ Reactive strategies optimize what already exists, while proactive measures build sustainability into what is being developed.

Reactive strategies act as emergency solutions - they aim to minimize the harmful effects of the IT technologies we already use. When sustainability issues are discovered or unexpected consequences arise, these strategies come into play to mitigate the effects. Proactive strategies can be compared to a gardener carefully planning a plantation that can create long-term value. It's about developing new technologies and processes with sustainability in mind so that we are ready for the challenges of the future.

![Figure 3.7](resources/I_3.7.png)
Figure 3.7. In the interaction of reactive and proactive strategies, more sustainable information technology can be developed.

By combining reactive and proactive strategies, organizations can strengthen their sustainability. It is crucial to use both approaches simultaneously, as shown in Figure 3.7, as they complement each other and create a coherent strategy for sustainability.

Reactive strategies help reduce the negative impacts of current technologies, while proactive strategies focus on integrating sustainability from the beginning to avoid future problems. New processes and products should be designed with sustainability in mind, while also continuously improving what we already have. By balancing both approaches, we can both solve today's challenges and ensure a more sustainable future.

## Summary: What can you take away from this chapter?

In this chapter, we have looked at how the hardware, networks and software of IT systems affect the environment, society and individuals, and how digitalization creates new problems and challenges - but also contributes to sustainable solutions. The chapter has also highlighted concrete examples - such as satellite pollution - to illustrate the long-term consequences of technological growth without regard for the environment. In addition, we have discussed different strategies, such as lifecycle management and circular processes , that can promote sustainable IT practices.

Sustainable hardware requires responsible sourcing of materials, recycling and proper waste management. When it comes to networking, sustainability is about reducing energy consumption and CO2 emissions from data centers. Transitioning from copper cables to fiber optics and locating data centers in areas with renewable energy are important steps. Efficient software optimizes the energy consumption of IT systems and can promote sustainability by optimizing processes and guiding users in sustainable practices. Software should also be designed with both the environment and user well-being in mind as much as possible to avoid issues such as digital addiction.

We had wanted to write about sustainable digitalization but realized along the way that 100% sustainable information technology is not something that exists yet. However, work is being done on many fronts to create a more sustainable technological world, where unsustainable technologies with many unintended side effects on the environment and society are transformed into "green", less polluting technologies. There is still a long way to go and a vast and important work ahead of us.

To move beyond the unsustainable IT solutions of the past, we should work with both reactive and proactive sustainability measures. It is essential that both strategies are used in mutual interaction, so that new processes and products are based on sustainability, while existing processes are continuously optimized.
